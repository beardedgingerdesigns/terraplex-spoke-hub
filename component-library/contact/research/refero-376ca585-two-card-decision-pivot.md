## Why this is library-worthy
A two-card decision pivot at the top of a contact page that routes intent BEFORE asking for a form: one card for "talk to sales," one for "get product / account support," each with its own CTA. The form-or-form-link shows up only after the user has self-segmented. Below the pivot, an offices grid (multi-column dense text) doubles as a credibility move and a fallback for users who want a phone number, not a form.

## What to take
- Side-by-side two-card row. Same width, same internal padding, same vertical content order: avatar/icon → bold heading → 2-line description → full-width primary CTA. The cards are visually identical in chrome; only the icon and copy differ.
- Cards sit in front of a large-format prop photograph (a real desk scene, not a stock office shot) that occupies the right ~50% of the hero band — it provides texture without a video and without an autoplay loop.
- "Contact us" headline is huge and left-aligned, spanning the row above the cards. No subhead — the cards themselves explain the choice.
- Below the pivot, an "Our offices around the world" section is a multi-column flat text grid (4–5 cols on desktop), each entry: location name, address lines, link. No cards, no icons — pure typography. Dense by design; trust comes from sheer specificity.
- Right of the offices grid, a second prop photograph echoes the hero photo (same prop family, different angle) — visually frames the address block without competing with it.

## What NOT to take
- Don't import the cheerful cartoon avatar icons (orange face, pink robot face) — they're brand-specific to the source. A neutral monochrome icon (envelope, chat bubble, headset) keeps the decision pivot legible across archetypes.
- The retro-telephone prop photo is the source's brand voice. Don't import the literal photo — adapt the photographic-prop concept (drone hardware on a workbench for a Terraplex dealer; real equipment, lit warmly).
- Don't replace the offices grid with a Google-Maps embed if you have multiple locations — the dense-typography grid scales to 20+ entries; a map embed maxes out usable detail at ~5 pins.

## Adaptation notes (cross-archetype)
- CLEAN: native fit — white background, dark teal/navy CTAs, neutral icons. Prop photo can be a clean equipment shot with soft drop shadow.
- FIELD: warm cream background, amber accent on CTAs. Prop photo is a warm-lit drone-on-fenceline or workbench-with-tools shot — keep it photographic, not graphic.
- TERRAIN: works as the "light band" version. Pair with a dark-band section above or below to maintain the alternating rhythm.
- FORGE / TACTICAL: invert the cards onto a charcoal or near-black background with white card surfaces (or near-black cards with rim lighting). Replace prop photo with a moody equipment hero. CTAs stay monochrome with an ember (FORGE) or cyan (TACTICAL) accent.
- PATRIOT: cards on a cream background with a flag-stripe accent rule above the offices grid. Card icons can use a single navy / red accent. The structure itself doesn't conflict with flag motifs because the chrome is restrained.

## Source brief
Zendesk's contact page — a two-pivot pattern (sales vs support) plus a global offices list. The library-worthy move is the intent-routing structure itself, not Zendesk's specific copy or props. Maps cleanly to a Terraplex dealer's split between equipment-purchase inquiries and service/compliance inquiries (e.g. Sam Welton's [Part 137 services](../../../content/compliance-program.md)).
