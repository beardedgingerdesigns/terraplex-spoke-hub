## Why this is library-worthy
A help / contact pattern where the **primary affordance is a conversational AI assistant chatbox** with example-question quick-pick chips, and traditional contact channels (email / phone / form) are relegated to a single small "Contact support" tile at the bottom as the human-fallback. Different from existing contact patterns: the [search-led help center](./refero-00cda269-search-led-help-center.md) leads with a search bar + 4-card category-routing grid + FAQ accordion (catalog-style); this pattern leads with a chat surface (conversational-style). Also includes a small "All systems operational" status indicator in the upper-right of the chat panel — a credibility-by-uptime move that signals "the system you're about to chat with is actually running." Right pick when the dealer has a real knowledge-base + LLM stack (or even a simple FAQ bot) and the customer's hesitation is "I don't want to read through 12 articles, I just want to ask my question."

## What to take
- Single full-page card layout (~80vh, contained inside a centered max-width column ~700px wide). Page background is plain white or very pale gray; the chat card is the only visual weight.
- Top of the card carries a small two-element row: program name on the left (~16px sentence-case bold) and a real-time status indicator on the right — small green dot (~8px) followed by "All systems operational" in a muted green text. The status indicator is the credibility opener — proves the bot is actually online.
- Below the program-name row, the chat card itself: white surface, ~16px corner radius, soft drop shadow (~12px blur, ~6% opacity), no visible border. Internal padding ~32px.
- Card header: bold sentence-case title ("Ask us anything" → for a dealer "Ask the [dealer-name] assistant"), a small info icon (?) in the upper-right corner of the card.
- Card body: an opening assistant message displayed as a left-aligned chat bubble — small avatar (~32px circle with the brand's monochrome logo glyph) on the left, message text on the right. Message is 2–3 sentences ("I'm an AI assistant trained on Resend's documentation, knowledge base, and other content. Ask me anything about Resend." → for a dealer "I'm a [dealer-name] assistant trained on the R-32 / I-19 documentation, Part 137 procedures, and dealer service catalog. Ask me anything about the equipment, compliance, or service.").
- Below the opening message, "Example questions" label (~12px muted), then 3–4 quick-pick chip buttons. Each chip is a thin pill (~36px tall) with a single short example question ("What if my domain is not verifying?" / "How do I fix CORS issues?" / "How do I set up DMARC?" → for a dealer "Which drone fits my acreage?" / "How do I prep for a Part 137 audit?" / "What's the trade-in value for my 2-year-old R-32?" / "Can I rent before I buy?"). Chips are click-to-send — clicking the chip submits that question into the chat.
- Below the chips, a chat input field — full-width, ~44px tall, light-gray background, no visible border, single placeholder ("How do I get started?"). A small send-arrow icon anchors the right edge of the field. Pressing Enter or clicking the arrow submits.
- Below the chat card, a single "Contact support" fallback tile — a thin horizontal bar (~64px tall) with bold "Contact support" title on the left, 1-line subhead beneath it ("Contact support for any questions or issues you may have."), and a small filled outline-pill CTA on the right ("Contact Support"). The tile is the human-fallback — the contact form / phone / email lives behind that one CTA. The tile is INTENTIONALLY small relative to the chat card; the visual hierarchy signals "try the assistant first, then fall back to a human."

## What NOT to take
- Don't add this pattern without a real working AI assistant or chatbot stack. A chat box that returns scripted "Thanks for your question, we'll get back to you" responses collapses the entire trust register the pattern is built on — the customer learns the assistant is fake within 1 message and the "All systems operational" indicator becomes an outright lie. If the dealer doesn't have a real knowledge-base + LLM stack OR a simple FAQ bot with real branching logic, use the [search-led help center](./refero-00cda269-search-led-help-center.md) (which is honest about being a catalog) instead.
- Don't omit the example-question chips. The chips are the page's onboarding affordance — they teach the customer what kinds of questions the assistant can actually answer. Without chips, the empty input field reads as "ask any question and we'll see what happens" which sets the bar too high; the chips constrain the expected question shape and therefore deliver more satisfying answers.
- Don't import Resend's literal example questions ("What if my domain is not verifying?", "How do I fix CORS issues?") — those are technical SaaS-product-specific. The transferable move is "3–4 chip prompts that match the dealer's actual most-common customer questions." For a Terraplex dealer this means real questions the dealer's knowledge-base can actually answer — equipment fit, Part 137 procedures, trade-in mechanics, service availability — calibrated to the dealer's actual support corpus.
- Don't skip the status indicator. The "All systems operational" + green dot is the page's primary credibility marker — without it, the chat reads as decorative. If the dealer's assistant has real downtime windows, surface them honestly ("Maintenance window: 2 AM – 4 AM CT" with a yellow dot).
- Don't make the "Contact support" fallback tile larger than the chat card. The visual hierarchy is the affordance — chat-first, contact-second. Making the contact tile equally weighted (or larger) collapses the pattern into a generic chat-and-form combo where the contact form competes with the assistant for attention.
- Don't import this pattern as a public landing-page contact section if the assistant lives behind a login. Resend's chat is inside an authenticated app dashboard; for a public-facing dealer site the assistant must be accessible without login — anything else collapses the conversion register because customers expect public chats to be available immediately.
- Don't combine this with the [split-screen trust-curtain contact](./refero-7c41e8a8-split-screen-trust-curtain.md) on the same page. Both occupy the "primary contact surface" slot; trust-curtain handles high-hesitation prospects with a credibility pre-frame; this AI-assistant pattern handles pre-existing customers with operational questions. Different audience, different page job.

## Source brief
Resend's [/help](https://resend.com/help) page — the in-app help center for the email-API platform, organized around an LLM-trained AI assistant rather than a search + FAQ catalog. The library-worthy move is the structural cadence (small program-name + status indicator → centered chat card with opening assistant message + example-question chips + chat input → small "Contact support" fallback tile below the card), reusable for a Terraplex dealer with a real knowledge-base + LLM stack — most directly relevant for dealers operating at scale who've indexed their documentation, Part 137 procedures, and service catalog into a chatbot, OR for the Terraplex parent brand surfacing a "ask the Terraplex assistant" public chat that routes operational questions across the [49-location dealer network](../../../research/manufacturers/terraplex.md). The pattern's whole credibility move is "ask in plain English; we built this for you specifically." Without a real backend, the pattern damages trust faster than any other contact surface in the library.
