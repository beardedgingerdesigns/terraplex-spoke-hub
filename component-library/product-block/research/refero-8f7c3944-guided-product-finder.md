## Why this is library-worthy
A multi-step **guided product finder** wizard that surfaces a recommended product through 3–5 progressively narrower questions, anchored by a photo-tile use-case selector at step one. Distinct from every existing product-block pattern: the [head-to-head comparison](./refero-a1142f19-head-to-head-comparison.md) and [spec-comparison grid](./refero-713b66c0-spec-comparison-grid.md) ask the customer to evaluate side-by-side; the [live-preview configurator](./refero-23ebbc37-live-preview-configurator.md) assumes the customer already knows which model and is now choosing modules; the [services maintenance-program recommender](../../services/research/refero-d928f889-maintenance-program-recommender.md) is a single-screen widget for service-tier routing. None of those handle "I don't know what I need — ask me a few questions and tell me." This pattern fills the **product-side recommendation gap**: customer doesn't know whether they want an [R-32](../../../products/r-32.md) vs [I-19](../../../products/i-19.md) vs an accessory bundle, and the wizard walks them through 3–5 use-case questions ("What are you spraying? / How many acres? / Where do you store equipment?") to surface a recommendation card with photo + model name + price + Add-to-cart at the end.

## What to take
- **Container framing.** The wizard sits in a contained near-full-viewport panel with a single sticky title at top-left ("Find Your Perfect Setup" / for a dealer "Find the Right Drone for Your Operation"). The page chrome (header nav + footer) stays visible — the wizard is a step-block within the page, not a full-page takeover. No modal, no overlay, no separate URL per step.
- **Step 1 is photo-tile use-case selection.** A horizontal row of 5 equal-width tiles, each ~140×140px, each with a real lifestyle photograph of the use case + a 1–2-word label below ("Travel essentials / Photography gadgets / Fast charging for my phone / Wireless charging / Help me choose"). For a dealer: ("60+ acres row crops / Specialty / Orchards / Pasture spot-spray / Help me choose"). The tiles are clickable cards (single-select); the selected tile gets a subtle dark border or filled-corner indicator. The "Help me choose" tile is the explicit escape hatch for customers who can't categorize their own use case — without it, the wizard feels gatekept.
- **Steps 2–5 are flat radio or checkbox questions.** After step 1, each subsequent step is a simple plain-text question + 2–4 vertical radio rows ("Where do you charge? / Deskside-Bedside / On the go"). For a dealer: ("Where do you store equipment? / Heated indoor barn / Unheated outbuilding / Outdoor under cover"). No photos, no chrome — the radio rows are the affordance. This deliberate visual descent from photo-tiles (step 1) to plain radios (steps 2+) is the pattern's pacing trick: the photo-tiles are "easy entry," the radio rows are "you're committed now, give me 30 seconds of details."
- **Persistent Back / Continue affordances + progress bar.** At the bottom-left of every step: outline "Back" button + filled black "Continue" button. Below them, a thin horizontal progress bar (~600px wide, ~3px tall) that fills proportionally as the customer advances. The progress bar is the only chrome that quantifies "how much longer" — and that quantification is what keeps customers from bailing. No step counter ("step 2 of 5") — the bar carries the same information without forcing the dealer to commit to a fixed step count.
- **The recommendation card at the end is a single-product hero, not a comparison.** Final step swaps the radio panel for a single product card: large product photograph + model name + 2-line "Why this is right for you" rationale (referencing the customer's earlier answers — "You said 60+ acres of row crops in an unheated outbuilding, so we recommend...") + price + Add to cart. Optional: a small secondary "See all options" link below the card for customers who want to override the recommendation.
- **The wizard pattern lives at the bottom of a product category page**, not the top. The dealer's category page (e.g. `/drones/`) opens with a hero + lineup grid; the guided finder appears below the lineup as a "Not sure which one? Let us help" alternative entry. Customers who want to browse get the lineup; customers who want to be told get the wizard. Both surfaces route to the same product detail pages.

## What NOT to take
- **Don't make step 1 anything other than photo tiles.** The photo-tile entry IS the affordance — without it, the wizard reads as a generic survey and abandonment spikes. If the dealer doesn't have 4–5 real lifestyle photos of distinct use-cases (real spray operation in row crops, in orchard, in pasture, etc.), don't ship this pattern; use the [head-to-head comparison](./refero-a1142f19-head-to-head-comparison.md) instead.
- **Don't exceed 5 steps.** At 6+ steps the progress bar moves too slowly and customers bail. If the dealer's recommendation logic genuinely needs 6+ inputs, branch the wizard (after step 2 the question set diverges by use-case) rather than adding steps to the linear path. Most dealers can route between R-32 / I-19 / accessory bundles with 3–4 questions.
- **Don't skip the "Help me choose" escape tile on step 1.** Customers whose use case doesn't fit the dealer's named categories will bail rather than misclassify themselves. The escape tile routes them to a slightly longer question path that infers use case from operational details.
- **Don't return more than 1 recommendation at the end.** A 2-card "here are your top 2 options, you decide" final step defeats the wizard's whole job (telling the customer what to buy). If the dealer can't commit to one recommendation, the [head-to-head comparison](./refero-a1142f19-head-to-head-comparison.md) is the right pattern, not this one.
- **Don't gate the rest of the page behind the wizard.** The wizard is an alternative path, not a required one. Customers who scroll past the lineup and reach the wizard chose to engage; forcing every visitor through it before showing product specs reads as a paywall.
- **Don't use the wizard for service-tier routing.** That's the [maintenance-program recommender](../../services/research/refero-d928f889-maintenance-program-recommender.md)'s job — a single-screen widget with model + flight-time inputs is the right shape for service tiers. The wizard pattern is for **product** routing where the customer doesn't yet have a model in mind.
- **Don't put a CTA in every step.** The Continue button is the only CTA that should advance state. A "Skip to product detail" CTA on step 2 collapses the wizard into a half-finished funnel.
- **Don't hide the progress bar on mobile.** Without it, mobile customers can't tell whether they're 1 step in or 4 steps in, and abandonment is steeper than desktop. Keep the bar at the same proportional width even on narrow viewports.
- **Don't put the wizard at the top of the category page.** Customers who already know what they want will perceive the wizard as friction. Put it below the lineup grid as the secondary entry path.

## Source brief
NomadGoods's [/pages/finder](https://nomadgoods.com/pages/finder) — a phone-accessory retailer (cases, chargers, cables) using a multi-step wizard to route customers from "I'm not sure what I need" to a product recommendation. The library-worthy move is the **photo-tile-led step-1 + plain-radio steps-2-onward + persistent Back/Continue + progress bar + single-product recommendation card at the end** structural cadence. Reusable for a Terraplex dealer's product-finder page where the customer doesn't yet know whether they want an R-32 vs an I-19 vs an accessory bundle, and a 3–5 question wizard ("What are you spraying? / How many acres? / How do you store equipment? / What's your annual budget?") routes them to a recommended product with rationale referencing their answers. Pairs naturally with category-page lineups: lineup grid at top for browsers, wizard at bottom for ask-to-be-told customers. Different from the [services maintenance-program recommender](../../services/research/refero-d928f889-maintenance-program-recommender.md) (single-screen widget, service-tier routing) — this is multi-step product routing.
