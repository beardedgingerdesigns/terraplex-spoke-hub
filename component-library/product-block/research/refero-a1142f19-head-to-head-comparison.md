## Why this is library-worthy
A head-to-head product-comparison page that helps a buyer disambiguate between 2–3 closely-related models in the same product line. The cadence is: question-headline hero → multi-section comparison table (camera / design / remote / flight performance, each as its own three-column table) with a hero photograph between every table that visually argues for the spec being compared → narrative "Summary of upgrades" wrap-up → bottom Buy combo cards for all three configurations side-by-side. Different from the [alternating-pastel-stripes](./refero-4bcd783f-alternating-pastel-stripes.md) (single product, multi-feature stripes), [dark-feature-bands](./refero-eb12db31-dark-feature-bands.md) (single product, cinematic bands), and [spec-sheet](./refero-27715bc5-spec-sheet-product-block.md) (single product, documentation-led) patterns — this is the right pick when the dealer carries multiple GTEEX models (e.g. [R-32](../../../products/r-32.md) and [I-19](../../../products/i-19.md)) and the customer's first question is "which one do I want?"

## What to take
- Open with a question-format headline ("R-32 vs. I-19: how do they compare?") set against a single muted-warm or muted-dark photo of one or both products in operating context — terrain or sky, not a studio sweep. The headline IS the page's value prop; no subheadline, no CTAs in the band.
- Below the hero, a three-column (or two-column) comparison table grouped by category. Each category gets its own bordered table block with a category heading above (e.g. "Camera Spec Comparison", "Design", "Flight Performance and Obstacle Sensing"). Column headers are the model names; rows are spec attributes (sensor, resolution, weight, range, etc.). Cells are flat text — no checkmarks, no icons, no color highlighting of "winning" specs. The reader does the comparison; the page just lays it out cleanly.
- Between every comparison table, a single full-bleed photograph that visually demonstrates the spec category just compared: between the camera table and the design table, a side-by-side photograph of the camera modules; between the flight-performance table and the design table, a hero shot of the drone in adverse conditions. Photos use cinematic golden-hour or moody-mountain lighting; no studio sweep. Each photo carries a small caption rail underneath ("Shot on R-32 / 24mm, f/2.8, ISO 100" type metadata) — caption credibility is the rhetorical move.
- Mid-page, after the spec tables, insert a "Benefits of [feature] system" or "How an [X] camera system differs" prose section: a single paragraph of plain-language explanation aimed at the customer who skimmed the tables and now wants to know what the specs MEAN. No bullet points; one or two paragraphs of conversational copy.
- Wrap with a "Summary of the upgrades of [Model A]" paragraph + 2–3 lifestyle photos — this is the closing argument before pricing. Photos are operator-and-equipment-in-context (operator launching, drone over crop, controller close-up) — not studio shots.
- Close with a "Pricing and Combos" band: 3 horizontal cards, one per model, each with a small product image, model name, configuration line ("Combo with 2 batteries / Fly More Combo"), price, and a Buy pill CTA. Cards are uniform — same height, same chrome, same CTA shape. No "RECOMMENDED" badges; the comparison above already did the recommendation work.

## What NOT to take
- Don't import the literal DJI camera lighting or mountain-and-snow location photography — that's DJI's brand. The transferable move is "cinematic full-bleed photograph that argues for the spec category just compared." For a Terraplex dealer that's golden-hour shots of the R-32 over corn rows, a side-by-side render of the R-32 and I-19 spreader configurations, an operator-at-controller shot in a working field — not stock alpine mountains.
- Don't highlight "winning" specs in green or use checkmark/×-mark cells. The pattern's credibility rests on letting the customer compare for themselves; visual nudging turns the page into a sales pitch and undermines the documentation register.
- Don't add a CTA between every table. The bottom pricing-combos band is the only conversion moment; mid-page CTAs compete with the comparison and rush the decision.
- Don't compare more than 3 models in a single page. The three-column table maxes out the eye's comparison capacity — at 4+ columns the table becomes a feature matrix and the photo interludes lose their per-spec argument.
- Don't drop the prose explanation paragraph. "Camera Spec Comparison" tables alone read as a feature matrix; the prose paragraph is what bridges specs to plain-language meaning for customers who don't already know what "4/3 CMOS Hasselblad" implies.
- Don't import the DJI navigation or breadcrumb chrome. The transferable artifact is the hero → table → photo → table cadence + bottom buy combos, not the surrounding e-commerce framing.

## Adaptation notes (cross-archetype)
- FIELD: cream background, warm-charcoal table cells with thin warm-cream rules between rows, photo interludes are golden-hour drone-in-field shots (operator launching at sunrise, drone tracking over corn rows). Question headline in warm-charcoal with a single amber accent on the model names in the headline. Pricing combo card CTAs are amber pills. Pair with the [manifesto-creed hero](../../hero/research/refero-b86571f2-manifesto-creed-hero.md) for a heritage-led opening before the comparison. See [FIELD archetype](../../../archetypes/field.md).
- CLEAN: native fit. White background, neutral-gray table rules, photos are clean equipment shots with soft drop shadows on flat backdrops. Single brand accent on the bottom-band CTAs only. See [CLEAN archetype](../../../archetypes/clean.md).
- TERRAIN: works as a long alternating-rhythm sequence — light-band table + dark-band photo interlude + light-band table + dark-band photo interlude. The photo interludes are the page's "dark band" moments by definition (cinematic photography reads dark even on a light background); use them as the rhythm anchors with topographic dividers. See [TERRAIN archetype](../../../archetypes/terrain.md).
- PATRIOT: cream background, deep-navy table headings and category labels, charcoal cell text. Photo interludes are American-farmland golden-hour operator/equipment shots — no stock alpine landscapes. Pricing combo card CTAs alternate navy and barn-red across the three model cards (e.g. R-32 navy, I-19 barn-red, combo navy) — that's the PATRIOT restraint move at the conversion moment. The question headline can lean heritage ("Built in the Americas, for the American Farmer — which one fits your operation?"). Pair with the [manifesto-creed hero](../../hero/research/refero-b86571f2-manifesto-creed-hero.md). See [PATRIOT archetype](../../../archetypes/patriot.md).
- FORGE: invert to a near-black background with charcoal table cells and white text; thin ember-warm rules between rows. Photo interludes lean into ember rim-lighting and dusk/forge-glow palettes — the hardware photographed at golden hour with a warm rim light feels native here. Pricing combo card CTAs are ember-orange pills, only place the accent shows up. Pair with the [hardware-lineup dark hero](../../hero/research/refero-0945a36c-hardware-lineup-dark-hero.md) above the comparison. See [FORGE archetype](../../../archetypes/forge.md).
- TACTICAL: native fit with tighter geometry — sharper rectangle CTAs (~6px radius) on the pricing combo cards, monochrome black/white only with a single steel-cyan accent on the model names in the question headline and the CTA buttons. Photo interludes are studio-shot product renders on seamless black, not environmental shots — the absence of context IS the affordance. Tables get a 1px steel-cyan border around the active column on hover (web-only enhancement). See [TACTICAL archetype](../../../archetypes/tactical.md).
