## Summary

On **May 28, 2026** DJI published the findings of an independent cybersecurity assessment conducted by **OnDefend** — a U.S.-based firm staffed by former military and government security professionals — on the **DJI Air 3S (with RC 2 controller)** and the **DJI Matrice 4E (with RC Plus 2 Enterprise controller)**. The assessment, conducted **October 2025 through March 2026**, reported **zero critical, zero high-risk, and zero medium-risk findings**, no hidden backdoors, no data transmissions outside the United States, no viable pathways for hijacking or weaponization, no unexplained RF emissions (full-spectrum scan 1 MHz–6 GHz), and no supply-chain tampering; **ten low-risk findings** and **thirteen observations** were noted, primarily on application security configurations and wireless hardening. DJI simultaneously launched the refreshed **DJI Trust Center** and **Drone Security White Paper V3**, packaging the OnDefend report alongside prior third-party assessments (FTI 2024 & 2020, Booz Allen 2020, INL 2019, DOI 2019, Kivu 2018) and compliance certifications (FIPS 140-2, ISO 27001, SOC 2). On approximately **June 1–2, 2026**, **sUAS News** published an analytical breakdown ("Inside the DJI Trust Center") finding that the broader compliance portfolio contains substantive gaps — the FIPS 140-2 certification is for the "DJI Core Crypto Engine" at **CMVP Level 1** (the lowest tier; Booz Allen had separately found the datalink crypto was not FIPS-validated), the SOC 2 (2017) listing has **no document** at all, the FTI 2020 entry resolves to an **HTTP 404** dead link on DJI's own site — characterizing the package as "a curated compliance portfolio with good production values" rather than a body of adversarial independent research. None of the new artifacts cover the **Agras line specifically**: the OnDefend scope was Air 3S + Matrice 4E.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers

This is DJI's **most substantive empirical pushback** to the FCC Covered List rationale since the [December 22, 2025 ruling](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md), and it lands in a procedural lull: the FCC reply-comment window on the [DJI Petition for Reconsideration](../digests/2026-05-11.md) (ET Docket 26-22) and [Autel Application for Review](../digests/2026-05-09.md) (ET Docket 26-23) closed May 11, 2026, the agency has not committed to a ruling timeline, and DJI's Ninth Circuit Case 26-1029 remains in requested abeyance through approximately November 2026. The OnDefend assessment is the kind of artifact DJI's counsel can cite to argue the Covered List rests on classified intelligence that empirical adversarial testing cannot reproduce — the audit specifically reports **no clear evidence of hidden backdoors, no data transmissions outside the United States, and no viable pathways for hijacking or weaponization**. For the **dealer-customer "isn't DJI actually a security risk?"** conversation, this is now the freshest data point on DJI's side of the ledger, and dealers should know it exists.

But three caveats matter for the [DJI Agras profile](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) specifically: **(1) the audit did not cover the Agras line**. OnDefend examined a consumer-class drone (Air 3S) and a commercial mapping platform (Matrice 4E). No Agras T-series airframe was in scope. The audit's findings cannot be transferred to the [T10 / T20 / T30 / T25 / T50](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) — those platforms have different radio stacks, different telemetry pathways, and different firmware update mechanisms than the audited consumer/enterprise drones. **(2) OnDefend itself flagged the snapshot caveat**: "Security tests are a snapshot which provide detailed information on what an attacker can do once they take advantage of that first vulnerability." A clean October-2025-to-March-2026 audit does not guarantee a clean April-onward firmware-update path. **(3) The sUAS News analytical breakdown highlights that the broader compliance portfolio has documented gaps** — FIPS 140-2 at Level 1, a missing SOC 2 document, a dead-link FTI 2020 entry on DJI's own site. The fair characterization for a dealer is "DJI has put forward a real adversarial-testing data point against the Covered List rationale, but the audit covers consumer/enterprise products rather than the Agras line, and the broader compliance portfolio is not as airtight as the Trust Center landing page implies."

The structural posture for Terraplex dealers is unchanged. The [2026-05-13 digest](2026-05-13.md) firmware-waiver extension to at least January 1, 2029 (FCC OET DA 26-454) is what protects the **installed-base patch pipeline** for existing T-series customers — that is the load-bearing operational protection, not the OnDefend audit. The [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md) Commissioner Trusty keynote signaling reconsideration is unlikely at Commission level is the load-bearing **forward-looking** protection on the "Built in the Americas" story. The OnDefend artifact is a meaningful new data point in DJI's empirical rebuttal but does not change the FCC procedural calendar, the Section 805 / 1260H supply-chain enforcement timeline (June 30, 2026 direct-procurement ban; June 30, 2027 indirect-supplier ban), or the absence of a confirmed DJI design win inside any U.S. ag-drone OEM's supply chain.

## Key facts

### OnDefend independent security assessment of DJI Air 3S + Matrice 4E (released May 28, 2026)

- **Assessment firm**: **OnDefend**, a U.S.-based cybersecurity firm trusted by national security stakeholders and enterprise leaders, staffed by former military and government security professionals [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/independent-assessment-clears-dji-drones-of-critical-national-security-risks/] [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment]
- **Scope**: two DJI drone systems — the **DJI Air 3S with RC 2 controller** and the **DJI Matrice 4E with RC Plus 2 Enterprise controller** — examined over a **five-month period from October 2025 through March 2026** [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/independent-assessment-clears-dji-drones-of-critical-national-security-risks/]
- **Methodology**: full-spectrum RF scanning from **1 MHz to 6 GHz**, hardware teardowns, silicon-level analysis, meddler-in-the-middle attacks, firmware testing, privilege escalation attempts, replay attacks, jamming simulations, and network interception exercises against hardware, firmware, apps, radio frequencies, supply chain components, and network traffic [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/28/dji-ondefend-security-audit-air-3s-matrice-4e/]
- **Findings — zero on the top tiers**: **zero critical findings, zero high-risk findings, zero medium-risk findings** across the full adversarial-testing scope [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/28/dji-drone-ban-security-report/] [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/independent-assessment-clears-dji-drones-of-critical-national-security-risks/]
- **Findings — lower-tier counts**: **ten low-risk findings** and **thirteen observations** primarily related to application security configurations and wireless hardening [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment]
- **Specific exclusions in the findings**: "no clear evidence of hidden backdoors, no data transmissions outside the United States, and no viable pathways for hijacking or weaponization"; no unexplained RF emissions (all detected signals traced to known system functions; emissions not previously documented in FCC filings were confirmed to be standard artifacts of signal generation methods, not covert channels); no supply chain tampering or unauthorized hardware modifications [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/independent-assessment-clears-dji-drones-of-critical-national-security-risks/] [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/28/dji-ondefend-security-audit-air-3s-matrice-4e/]
- **OnDefend snapshot caveat on record**: OnDefend states plainly that "Security tests are a snapshot which provide detailed information on what an attacker can do once they take advantage of that first vulnerability" [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]
- **Scope exclusion — Agras line not in scope**: the OnDefend assessment examined Air 3S (consumer) and Matrice 4E (commercial enterprise mapping); no [DJI Agras T-series](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) airframe was tested [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment]

### DJI Trust Center + Drone Security White Paper V3 launch (May 28, 2026)

- DJI simultaneously launched the **DJI Trust Center** refresh and the **Drone Security White Paper V3** in concert with the OnDefend release, packaging the OnDefend report alongside prior third-party assessments and compliance certifications [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-launches-trust-center] [source: https://www.dji.com/trust-center/resource/white-paper]
- **Substantive prior assessments listed** (per sUAS News reading of the Trust Center): FTI 2024 & 2020, Booz Allen 2020, Idaho National Laboratory (INL) 2019, Department of the Interior (DOI) 2019, Kivu 2018 — all of which are real network / RF / data-flow tests, alongside the new 2026 OnDefend report [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]
- **Compliance certifications listed**: NIST FIPS 140-2 certification for the "DJI Core Crypto Engine" + ISO 27001 certification for DJI's FlightHub 2 fleet management solution + SOC 2 [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-launches-trust-center]

### sUAS News analytical breakdown — gaps in DJI's compliance portfolio (published ~June 1–2, 2026)

- **sUAS News** published "Inside the DJI Trust Center" in **June 2026** providing an analytical breakdown finding "a massive gap between technical reality and marketing spin" and characterizing the package as "a curated compliance portfolio with good production values" rather than a body of adversarial independent research that clears DJI of national-security concerns [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]
- **FIPS 140-2 gap**: the certification is for the "DJI Core Crypto Engine" at **CMVP Level 1** — the lowest of the four CMVP tiers — and Booz Allen had separately found the datalink crypto was not FIPS-validated [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]
- **SOC 2 gap**: the SOC 2 (2017) listing on the Trust Center has **no document at all** — "a claim, not an artifact" [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]
- **FTI 2020 gap**: the FTI 2020 entry resolves to an **HTTP 404 dead link** on DJI's own site [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]
- **The genuinely substantive core** per sUAS News: OnDefend (2026), FTI (2024 & 2020), Booz Allen (2020), INL (2019), DOI (2019), and Kivu (2018) — but each of these is a narrow, time-boxed penetration test, and OnDefend's own snapshot caveat is the framing sUAS News uses to argue these point-in-time results cannot be presented as standing guarantees of national security [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/06/inside-the-dji-trust-center/]

## Implications

- **For dealer-customer "isn't DJI actually a security risk?" conversations**: the honest post-May-28 framing is "DJI commissioned an adversarial security assessment from OnDefend, a U.S. cybersecurity firm staffed by former military and government security professionals; over five months of testing on the Air 3S and Matrice 4E (October 2025–March 2026), OnDefend reported zero critical/high/medium-risk findings, no hidden backdoors, no data leaving the United States, and no viable hijacking pathways. That is a real data point. But three caveats matter: the audit did **not** cover the Agras agricultural line; OnDefend itself describes its testing as a 'snapshot' that doesn't guarantee future firmware paths; and sUAS News' analytical breakdown of DJI's broader Trust Center compliance portfolio found documented gaps including FIPS 140-2 only at CMVP Level 1, a missing SOC 2 document, and a dead-link FTI 2020 entry. The Pentagon's classified intelligence opposition memo (April 10, 2026) was not addressed by this audit and remains in the record." Do **not** claim the OnDefend audit "cleared the Agras line" — it did not.
- **For the [DJI Agras profile](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md)** — when the profile next refreshes (monthly intelligence pass cadence), add a section noting (a) DJI commissioned an OnDefend cybersecurity assessment of Air 3S + Matrice 4E released May 28, 2026 with the headline findings above; (b) the audit scope explicitly excluded the Agras T-series; (c) the sUAS News critical analytical breakdown of DJI's Trust Center / White Paper V3 packaging; (d) the OnDefend artifact is now publicly available for DJI's counsel to cite in any future Ninth Circuit Case 26-1029 briefing or FCC reconsideration follow-up filing.
- **For the regulatory tracking record** (the [2026-04 regulatory update](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md)): the OnDefend audit is a meaningful evidentiary data point in the ongoing Covered List proceeding, but it does **not** change the procedural posture — the FCC reply-comment window closed May 11, the agency has not committed to a ruling timeline, the Ninth Circuit case remains in requested abeyance, and the Section 805 enforcement calendar (June 30, 2026 direct-procurement ban; June 30, 2027 indirect-supplier ban) is unchanged.
- **For Sam Welton's [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md) curriculum**: this is **not** a curriculum item — the OnDefend assessment is about FCC Covered List rationale, not Part 137 or Part 107 operational compliance. Prep-course instructors who get the question "but didn't DJI just pass a security audit?" can reference the three caveats above (scope excludes Agras; snapshot caveat from OnDefend itself; sUAS News finding documented gaps in the broader compliance portfolio).
- **For onboarding scripts** when prospects ask about the durability of the "Built in the Americas" structural advantage: the accurate post-May-28 answer is that the OnDefend artifact strengthens DJI's empirical pushback story but does not change the FCC procedural calendar, does not address the Agras line, and does not unwind the Section 805 / 1260H supply-chain enforcement timeline. The structural advantage holds; this is an evidentiary skirmish inside an ongoing proceeding, not an inflection point.
- **For monitoring follow-up**: watch (a) any **FCC procedural action** on ET Docket 26-22 or 26-23 (a tentative ruling, an extension, or a Commission-level reconsideration grant would be the highest-impact event); (b) any **DJI follow-up audit** that explicitly includes the Agras T-series in scope — that would be the artifact that actually addresses the ag-drone-dealer conversation; (c) any **counter-filing or counter-analysis** from the Pentagon, the Commercial Drone Alliance, or the Department of War in response to the OnDefend findings; (d) any **Section 805 1260H listing update or notification-pilot publication** since Cadenazzi's May 12 XPONENTIAL keynote (see [2026-05-14 digest](2026-05-14.md)) confirmed contractor-notification pilots begin during 2026 ahead of the June 30 ban.
