## Summary
The FCC's Office of Engineering and Technology (OET) released **Public Notice DA 26-454 on Friday, May 8, 2026**, extending the existing firmware-and-software-update waiver for previously authorized foreign-made drones, UAS critical components, and consumer-grade routers from the prior January 1, 2027 cutoff out to **at least January 1, 2029** — a two-year extension that was issued three days before the reply-comment deadline on the DJI/Autel Petitions for Reconsideration (the proceeding captured in the [2026-05-08](2026-05-08.md), [2026-05-09](2026-05-09.md), and [2026-05-11](2026-05-11.md) digests). DA 26-454 also **broadens** the waiver from the Class I permissive-change scope of the prior order to also cover **Class II permissive changes** — substantive software and firmware modifications that mitigate consumer harm, patch critical vulnerabilities, and maintain operating-system / network compatibility. OET signaled in the final paragraph that it intends to "as soon as practicable, recommend to the full Commission that it consider codifying this waiver" via rulemaking, which would convert the current administrative waiver into a regulation that requires a notice-and-comment proceeding to undo.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers
This is the first 2026 FCC action that **materially extends the operational runway of the installed DJI Agras fleet** without reopening the new-equipment authorization door. The [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md) captured Commissioner Trusty's May 7 speech defending the original Covered List ruling and concluded "the operating assumption for the rest of 2026 and into 2027 is that no new DJI Agras model will be FCC-authorized for US sale or import" — DA 26-454 leaves that conclusion completely intact (the waiver explicitly **does not** remove devices from the Covered List or reverse the broader restrictions), but it removes the parallel fear that customer-owned DJI Agras T40 / T50 / T70P airframes would be cut off from firmware patches in the spray season after this one. The installed Agras fleet now has a credible patch pipeline through the **end of the 2028 spray season**, not just through 2026.

For dealer-customer conversations, this is the news that resolves the most concrete "what about my existing fleet" question dealers in DJI-heavy territories (Iowa, Nebraska, Kansas, Missouri, Illinois — see the [competitor distributors profile](../dealer-economics/competitor-distributors.md)) have been getting since December. The honest framing was previously "your existing T-series airframes will keep flying, but firmware support is unclear past early 2027." The honest framing now is "your existing T-series airframes will keep flying and will receive security patches and substantive firmware updates through at least January 2029, and the FCC has signaled it intends to codify that via rulemaking rather than leave it as a revocable waiver." That is a stronger answer to give a farmer deciding whether to keep operating a 2024-or-earlier Agras for the next two spray seasons. It does **not** change the "Built in the Americas, for the American Farmer" positioning around the [GTEEX Revolution lineup](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) and [Hylio](../manufacturers/hylio.md) hardware — that positioning is about the **new-equipment** authorization landscape, which DA 26-454 leaves untouched. The dealer pitch evolves to a sharper two-part frame: "your existing Agras fleet has a 2029+ firmware runway, and your next airframe comes from a domestic OEM with a clean regulatory path."

## Key facts

### FCC DA 26-454 extends foreign-drone firmware waiver to at least January 1, 2029 and broadens scope to Class II permissive changes

- The FCC's **Office of Engineering and Technology** released **Public Notice DA 26-454 on Friday, May 8, 2026**, extending the waiver for software and firmware updates to previously authorized foreign-made drones, UAS critical components, and consumer-grade routers from prior deadlines (January 1, 2027 for drones; March 1, 2027 for routers) to **at least January 1, 2029** [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf] [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/fcc-grants-two-year-software-lifeline-for-dji-drone-and-tp-link-router-owners/] [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/]
- The waiver **broadens** the prior scope: previously it covered only **Class I permissive changes**; DA 26-454 now also covers **Class II permissive changes** — substantive software and firmware modifications that mitigate consumer harm, including updates that ensure continued device functionality, patch critical vulnerabilities, and maintain compatibility with varying operating systems and network environments [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/] [source: https://www.tomshardware.com/networking/routers/fcc-reverses-course-allows-software-updates-for-foreign-made-drones-and-routers-until-2029-agency-says-blocking-security-patches-could-create-cybersecurity-risks]
- Scope of covered equipment: **all UAS, UAS critical components, and consumer-grade routers manufactured outside the United States that received FCC equipment authorization before being placed on the Covered List** [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf] [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/fcc-grants-two-year-software-lifeline-for-dji-drone-and-tp-link-router-owners/]
- FCC rationale: continued software support remains necessary to protect U.S. consumers; the public interest is better served by allowing these limited updates than by freezing software support entirely — the agency characterized blocking security patches as itself a cybersecurity risk [source: https://www.tomshardware.com/networking/routers/fcc-reverses-course-allows-software-updates-for-foreign-made-drones-and-routers-until-2029-agency-says-blocking-security-patches-could-create-cybersecurity-risks] [source: https://www.uasvision.com/2026/05/11/fcc-u-turn-allows-software-updates-for-foreign-drones-and-routers-until-2029/]
- **Codification signal**: the final paragraph of DA 26-454 states that OET will, *as soon as practicable, recommend to the full Commission that it consider codifying this waiver with respect to covered equipment described herein, as well as any future covered equipment with similar characteristics* — which would convert the firmware-update permission from a discretionary administrative decision (revocable by the next OET memo) into a codified regulation requiring a full notice-and-comment proceeding to undo [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/]
- **Important limitation**: the waiver does **not** remove [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md), Autel, XAG, or other foreign-manufactured UAS from the Covered List, and does not reverse the December 22, 2025 ruling captured in the [April 2026 regulatory roundup](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md); it applies only to **already-authorized products** and only to **software- and firmware-related changes** intended to maintain safe and secure operation [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf]
- **Timing context**: the May 8 release date sits three days before the May 11 reply-comment deadline on the DJI Petition for Reconsideration (ET Docket 26-22) and Autel Application for Review (ET Docket 26-23) — issued during the same week that Commissioner Trusty publicly defended the Covered List rationale at the SCSP AI+ Expo (see the [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md)), reinforcing that the agency is distinguishing the **installed-base patch question** from the **new-equipment authorization question** and resolving them in opposite directions (context: original Covered List ruling occurred 2025-12-22; prior firmware waiver issued under DA 26-69 with a 2027 cutoff)
- Multi-source corroboration on the substantive facts: sUAS News (on the [research/](../README.md) source allowlist), DroneXL, UAS Vision, Tom's Hardware, and the underlying FCC document itself all carry the same release-date, deadline-extension, and scope-broadening specifics [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/fcc-grants-two-year-software-lifeline-for-dji-drone-and-tp-link-router-owners/] [source: https://www.uasvision.com/2026/05/11/fcc-u-turn-allows-software-updates-for-foreign-drones-and-routers-until-2029/]

## Implications

- **For dealer-customer conversations about installed-base longevity**: the honest framing changes from "your existing Agras T-series airframes will keep flying, but firmware support is unclear past early 2027" to "your existing T-series airframes will keep flying and will receive security patches plus substantive firmware updates through at least January 1, 2029, and OET has signaled it intends to recommend the full Commission codify this via rulemaking." This resolves the most concrete fleet-longevity question dealers have been hedging on since the December 22, 2025 Covered List ruling and lets the dealer commit to a multi-year operational horizon for the installed fleet without speculation.
- **For positioning copy on the GTEEX Revolution / Hylio lineup**: do **not** weaken the "Built in the Americas, for the American Farmer" message around the [GTEEX Revolution](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) and [Hylio](../manufacturers/hylio.md) hardware — DA 26-454 explicitly does not reverse the Covered List or restore the new-DJI-authorization pathway. The dealer pitch evolves to a sharper two-part frame: "your existing Agras fleet has a 2029+ firmware runway under the FCC's May 8 waiver extension, and your next airframe comes from a domestic OEM ([GTEEX Revolution](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) / [Hylio](../manufacturers/hylio.md)) with a clean regulatory path." The new-equipment landscape is unchanged; the installed-base patch landscape is improved.
- **For Sam Welton's [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md) curriculum**: the post-May-11 module pivot already flagged in the [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md) should now include a DA 26-454 segment. - **For onboarding scripts**: when prospects ask "what happens to my existing DJI drone in two years?", the accurate answer post-DA-26-454 is "the FCC extended firmware support through at least January 2029 — your existing equipment keeps getting security patches and substantive software updates for at least three more spray seasons.
