## Summary

**Autel Robotics filed its reply brief in FCC ET Docket 26-23 on Monday, May 11, 2026**, the same end-of-day Petition-for-Reconsideration deadline the parallel DJI docket 26-22 closed on (context: deadline previewed in the [2026-05-08 digest](2026-05-08.md) and [2026-05-09 digest](2026-05-09.md)). Trade-press coverage of the filing landed Tuesday, May 19, 2026, across **dronexl.co**, **dronedj.com**, and **dronelife.com** — three-domain corroboration on the same date — with follow-on coverage running on dronexl.co's weekly drone-news roundup May 22 and on dronedj.com's May 28 readout that **comment volume on the combined DJI/Autel proceeding now exceeds 3,000 public comments, roughly 10× a typical FCC docket of comparable scope**. Two substantive moves distinguish the Autel reply from a generic procedural defense: **(1)** Autel argues the FCC's Covered-List determination rests on classified material Autel was never permitted to see and on factual allegations actually aimed at DJI, invoking the **2014 D.C. Circuit ruling Ralls Corp. v. CFIUS** (national-security review cannot rest on opaque category-wide assumptions without giving affected companies the unclassified portion of the record) and framing this as a Fifth Amendment due-process violation; **(2)** the reply walks through **sworn declarations describing Autel's data-handling architecture** — flight data stored locally by default and not auto-uploaded to company servers; communications and stored data encrypted with **AES-128 or AES-256**; no third party authorized to access drone-operation software or customer accounts — none of which appeared in the December 22, 2025 Public Notice that placed the company on the Covered List. The reply attacks by name the opposition brief filed by the **Foundation for American Innovation (FAI)**, arguing FAI leaned on DJI-specific controversies (AeroScope, mobile-app behavior, alleged telemetry collection) to justify keeping Autel on the list. Autel explicitly distances itself from DJI throughout — the procedural move signals Autel is positioning for **case-by-case relief** rather than waiting on a collective Covered-List reconsideration outcome.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers

Autel is **not an ag-spray-drone OEM** — the company's catalog is rotary-wing consumer/enterprise, fixed-wing surveying (Dragonfish), and public-safety platforms; it does not compete in the Part 137 heavy-payload spray segment that defines the dealer channel. But the reply is **dealer-relevant by procedural proxy**: the [DJI Agras profile's](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) revision-2026-05-01 baseline established that DJI's parallel petition in **ET Docket 26-22** is the single procedural lever that could reopen US authorizations for the unreleased **Agras T55** (currently blocked) and for replacement parts on the existing T30/T40/T50 fleet that dealers continue servicing under the **OET DA 26-454 firmware waiver out to January 1, 2029** (context: [2026-05-13 digest](2026-05-13.md)). Three procedural reads come out of the May 11 reply:

**First**, Autel's Ralls Corp. v. CFIUS argument — that the FCC must surface at least the unclassified portion of the record — applies with equal force to the DJI docket, because both petitions face the **Pentagon's April 10, 2026 classified-intelligence opposition memo** that was the structural anchor of the government's case (context: captured in the [2026-05-09 digest](2026-05-09.md)). If the Commission accepts the Ralls reasoning for Autel, the same logic creates daylight in DJI's docket. **Second**, by attacking the FAI opposition brief specifically — and by characterizing FAI's argument as DJI-borrowed — Autel is **inviting the Commission to disaggregate the two proceedings**. If Autel succeeds and DJI doesn't, the signal to the industry is that the FCC is willing to carve case-by-case relief, which strengthens the procedural template DJI's counsel can recycle in subsequent filings or in the Ninth Circuit appeal. **Third**, the sworn-declaration approach (specific encryption standards, local-by-default flight data, no third-party access) is a **substantive disclosure floor** the FCC has not previously had on the record for Autel — and DJI has visible incentive to make a parallel disclosure if the Commission signals it is responsive to that kind of evidence.

The honest dealer-customer framing is unchanged in operational terms — Commissioner Trusty's May 7 SCSP keynote remains the strongest first-Commissioner-statement signal that reconsideration at Commission level is unlikely (context: [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md)) — but the **procedural surface area for Covered-List relief has expanded** in ways prior-week framing didn't anticipate. The dealer message should remain: "your existing DJI Agras fleet has firmware support through January 1, 2029; new SKU authorizations remain blocked; the procedural reconsideration is ongoing but should not change your next-fleet planning assumptions." What's new this week is that **the comment-volume surge (3,000+ comments, ~10× normal) is itself an FCC political-cost signal** the Commission cannot ignore on a delegated-authority track, and the Autel Ralls argument is the first published filing that gives the FCC a defensible procedural off-ramp if it decides — for any reason — to revisit the December 22 ruling without conceding the underlying national-security premise.

## Key facts

### Autel reply brief — ET Docket 26-23, filed May 11, 2026 (reported May 19, 2026)

- **Filing date and docket**: Autel Robotics filed its reply on Monday, May 11, 2026, the end-of-day reply-comment deadline in **ET Docket 26-23** (Application for Review of the FCC Public Safety and Homeland Security Bureau's December 22, 2025 action adding Autel to the Covered List). The parallel DJI Petition for Reconsideration in **ET Docket 26-22** closed on the same deadline. Trade-press coverage of the Autel reply landed May 19, 2026 across three drone-trade outlets on the same date [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-reply-covered-list-secret-evidence-dji/] [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/19/autel-drone-ban-us-fcc/] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-covered-list-challenge/]
- **Ralls Corp. v. CFIUS precedent**: Autel cites the **2014 D.C. Circuit ruling Ralls Corp. v. CFIUS** for the proposition that even in national-security review, affected companies must be given access to at least the unclassified portion of the record against them and a meaningful opportunity to rebut. Autel argues none of those due-process safeguards were provided in the Covered-List determination [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-reply-covered-list-secret-evidence-dji/] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-covered-list-challenge/]
- **Fifth Amendment due-process framing**: Autel argues the Covered-List determination violates Fifth Amendment due-process protections because the company was given no meaningful notice or opportunity to respond before the regulatory consequence; the **Department of War confirmed that classified and undisclosed material played a role in the determination process** [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/19/autel-drone-ban-us-fcc/] [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-reply-covered-list-secret-evidence-dji/]
- **Sworn-declaration data-handling architecture** (new on the record): Autel filed sworn declarations stating that **(a)** flight data is **stored locally by default** and is not automatically uploaded to company servers; **(b)** drone communications and stored data use **AES-128 or AES-256 encryption**; **(c)** no third party has been authorized to access drone-operation software or customer accounts. None of this operational detail appeared in the December 22, 2025 Public Notice that placed Autel on the Covered List [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/19/autel-drone-ban-us-fcc/] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-covered-list-challenge/]
- **Direct attack on Foundation for American Innovation opposition**: Autel's reply names the **Foundation for American Innovation (FAI) opposition brief** and argues FAI leaned heavily on **DJI-specific controversies — AeroScope, mobile-app behavior, alleged telemetry collection** — and used them to justify keeping Autel on the list. Autel positions itself throughout the brief as distinct from DJI, inviting the Commission to disaggregate ET Docket 26-22 and ET Docket 26-23 outcomes [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-reply-covered-list-secret-evidence-dji/] [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/19/autel-drone-ban-us-fcc/]

### Combined-docket comment volume update (reported May 28, 2026)

- As of late May 2026 reporting, **comment volume in the combined DJI / Autel proceeding now exceeds 3,000 public comments — roughly 10× the typical volume of comparable FCC proceedings**. The dominant filer demographic is described as farmers; the [2026-05-09 digest's](2026-05-09.md) snapshot was 460+ comments as of 2026-05-07, so volume grew ~6.5× across the final four days of the comment window and the post-deadline interval [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/28/fcc-autel-dji-drone-ban/]
- This is consistent with the **Drone Service Providers Alliance / National Agricultural Aviation Association** mobilization captured in the [2026-05-09 digest](2026-05-09.md) (DSPA filing arguing FCC action "stripped operators of preferred toolset without clear domestic replacement") and continues the pattern of farmer / Part 107 / Part 137 / public-safety practitioner sign-on that has characterized the comment record from the outset

### Procedural posture — what is NOT decided

- The reply-comment deadline closed May 11, 2026; the Commission has not yet acted on either docket. **No timeline has been announced for a final Commission ruling** on the Petition for Reconsideration or the Application for Review [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/19/autel-fcc-covered-list-challenge/] [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/28/fcc-autel-dji-drone-ban/]
- The DA 26-454 firmware-waiver track (context: [2026-05-13 digest](2026-05-13.md)) remains procedurally distinct from the reconsideration dockets. **DA 26-454 does not modify the Covered List itself** and the Covered-List reconsideration outcome — whichever way it goes — does not retroactively affect the January 1, 2029 firmware-support horizon now in place for already-authorized DJI Agras airframes
- DJI's parallel Ninth Circuit Case 26-1029 (currently in abeyance briefing per the [2026-05-09 digest](2026-05-09.md)) remains procedurally separate from the FCC reconsideration dockets. The Autel reply is filed only at the FCC, not in any parallel Article III proceeding
