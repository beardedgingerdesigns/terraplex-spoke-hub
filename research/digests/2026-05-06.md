## Summary
The FAA published its long-delayed Section 2209 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in the Federal Register on May 6, 2026 (Document 2026-08943, Docket FAA-2026-4558, Notice 26-03), creating a process for fixed-site-facility operators to petition for a Standard Unmanned Aircraft Flight Restriction; proposed § 74.250 explicitly preserves transit access for drones operating under Part 137 (agricultural aircraft operations), Parts 91/107/108/135, provided they broadcast Remote ID and notify the facility per § 74.255 — public comments are due July 6, 2026. Separately, on May 5, 2026, Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton issued a Civil Investigative Demand to Florida-based drone retailer Drone Nerds, LLC under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act, escalating the February 2026 lawsuit against Anzu Robotics (alleged DJI-rebrand line) from the manufacturer to its US distribution channel — the first publicly-noticed state-level enforcement specifically targeting a drone *distributor* for misrepresenting product origin.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers
The Section 2209 NPRM is procedurally large but operationally narrow for ag-spray dealers: it does not change Part 137 itself, and it does not pull spray drones out of UAFR airspace — the explicit Part 137 carve-out in § 74.250 means a Part 137 operator with Remote ID and prior facility notification continues to have legal access to transit a Standard UAFR. That is a *defensive* win the ag-drone industry has been asking for since the 2016 statute; if the rule proceeds without that Part 137 exception being weakened in comment, ag-spray operators near critical-infrastructure facilities (refineries, power plants, data centers, prisons) retain the same operational footprint they have today. The dealer-actionable part is the comment window: Terraplex's compliance program (Sam Welton, see [content/compliance-program.md](../../content/compliance-program.md)) and the dealer network should know that the July 6 deadline is the leverage point — if a fixed-site-facility class lobby pushes to remove or narrow the Part 137 transit allowance during the 60-day comment period, that's where dealers and their customers can file. This is the highest-stakes ag-drone procedural rule of 2026 so far, and the [April regulatory roundup](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md) baseline (Part 108 still in process, Part 137 stable) should be updated when the comment period closes.

The Texas AG / Drone Nerds escalation is structurally relevant to Terraplex's distributor model in a less obvious way. The CID is not about the Anzu *drone* — it is about the *distributor's* statement that Anzu is "headquartered and operated within the United States" being misleading because the underlying product line is alleged to be a DJI-Mavic-3-Enterprise rebrand. The pattern Paxton is establishing is: **state AGs can use deceptive-trade-practices law to enforce country-of-origin and supply-chain claims against drone distributors**, even when the distributor itself is not the manufacturer. That precedent matters for every distributor in the ag-drone space — including Terraplex — that markets "Built in the Americas" or similar provenance language (see [content/positioning.md](../../content/positioning.md) for the canonical brand-language section). The Anzu/DJI story is not directly comparable to Terraplex/GTEEX (Anzu is alleged to be a covert China-supply-chain rebrand; GTEEX is publicly assembled in the Americas with documented [Revolution Drones](../../content/positioning.md) component sourcing), but it raises the bar on what dealer marketing copy needs to be able to substantiate — and it specifically validates that "headquartered and operated within the United States" language as a category of claim that state AGs are now willing to litigate over.

## Key facts

### Item 1: FAA publishes Section 2209 NPRM — Part 137 transit access preserved in proposed § 74.250
- The Notice of Proposed Rulemaking "Designation-Restrict the Operation of Unmanned Aircraft in Close Proximity to a Fixed Site Facility" was published in the Federal Register on May 6, 2026 as Document 2026-08943, Notice No. 26-03, under Docket FAA-2026-4558; it implements section 2209 of the FAA Extension, Safety, and Security Act of 2016, a decade after the statute directed FAA to create the petition process [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility] [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/05/faa-section-2209-nprm-drops/]
- The rule creates a new 14 CFR Part 74 establishing a "Standard Unmanned Aircraft Flight Restriction" (Standard UAFR) that fixed-site-facility owners (qualifying critical-infrastructure or fixed-site categories) can petition the FAA to designate around their facility [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility] [source: https://www.unmannedairspace.info/uncategorized/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-protecting-critical-infrastructure-from-rogue-drones/]
- Proposed § 74.250 explicitly allows drones operating under Parts 91, 107, 108, 135, **and 137** to transit a Standard UAFR, provided they (a) broadcast Remote ID under Part 89, (b) transit in the shortest practicable time, and (c) notify the facility per the procedures in proposed § 74.255 [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/05/faa-section-2209-nprm-drops/] [source: https://www.unmannedairspace.info/uncategorized/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-protecting-critical-infrastructure-from-rogue-drones/]
- FAA-required pre-designation review: before designating a Standard UAFR the FAA must assess any environmental and community effect; if the petition meets the criteria, the FAA publishes the proposal in the Federal Register for a minimum 30-day public comment period before the UAFR takes effect for that facility [source: https://www.unmannedairspace.info/uncategorized/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-protecting-critical-infrastructure-from-rogue-drones/]
- Comment period on the underlying NPRM: comments are due on or before **July 6, 2026** (60 days after Federal Register publication), filed via regulations.gov to Docket No. FAA-2026-4558, by mail to Docket Operations at 1200 New Jersey Avenue SE, by hand delivery, or by fax to (202) 493-2251 [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility] [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/05/faa-section-2209-nprm-drops/]
- Statutory background: section 2209 was enacted in 2016 and has been delayed multiple times (most recently a 2024 schedule slip per dronexl.co's reporting trail); this NPRM is the first formal rulemaking step toward operationalizing the statutory framework [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/05/faa-section-2209-nprm-drops/]

### Item 2: Texas AG issues Civil Investigative Demand to drone distributor Drone Nerds, LLC under Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act
- On May 5, 2026, Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton announced an investigation into Drone Nerds, LLC, a Florida-based drone retailer, by issuing a Civil Investigative Demand (CID) under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA); the CID asks for documents related to the company's operations and user-data handling [source: https://www.texasattorneygeneral.gov/news/releases/attorney-general-ken-paxton-investigates-drone-company-threatening-texans-security-and-privacy] [source: https://www.click2houston.com/news/local/2026/05/05/texas-ag-launches-investigation-into-drone-company-over-data-security-concerns/]
- Specific allegation: Drone Nerds markets the Anzu Robotics Raptor drone with the line that it is "headquartered and operated within the United States, giving you the peace of mind that your solution is delivered by your neighbors," which the AG's office contends is misleading because the Anzu Raptor is alleged to be a rebrand of the Chinese-manufactured DJI Mavic 3 Enterprise platform [source: https://www.texasattorneygeneral.gov/news/releases/attorney-general-ken-paxton-investigates-drone-company-threatening-texans-security-and-privacy] [source: https://texasscorecard.com/state/texas-ag-investigates-drone-distributor-tied-to-ccp-linked-company/]
- This is an *escalation*, not a new line of action: in February 2026 the same AG's office sued Anzu Robotics (the manufacturer/branding entity) on parallel deceptive-trade-practices grounds — the May 2026 CID extends the enforcement posture to Anzu's distribution channel for the first time (context: original Anzu lawsuit filed 2026-02-18) [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/02/texas-ag-ken-paxton-sues-anzu-robotics-over-alleged-dji-rebranding-scheme/] [source: https://www.click2houston.com/news/local/2026/02/18/texas-ag-ken-paxton-sues-anzu-robotics-over-alleged-china-linked-drones/]
- Corporate ownership context: in November 2025, XTI Aerospace acquired both Drone Nerds and Anzu Robotics — the entities now share a corporate parent, which the AG's office cites as additional evidence that the distributor and manufacturer are "likely operating together to evade Texas law" [source: https://www.texasattorneygeneral.gov/news/releases/attorney-general-ken-paxton-investigates-drone-company-threatening-texans-security-and-privacy] [source: https://texasscorecard.com/state/texas-ag-investigates-drone-distributor-tied-to-ccp-linked-company/]
- Scope caveat: the Anzu Raptor is an enterprise/inspection-class small UAS (DJI Mavic 3 Enterprise lineage), not a Part 137 ag-spray platform; the immediate product market overlap with [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) and ag-drone dealers is small. The structural read-through to ag-drone distributors is the legal pattern, not the product.
- Filing tempo: Paxton's office framed the CID as the "second major lawsuit against a CCP-aligned company" in the same week, signaling an active multi-target enforcement campaign rather than a one-off action [source: https://www.texasattorneygeneral.gov/news/releases/attorney-general-ken-paxton-investigates-drone-company-threatening-texans-security-and-privacy]

## Implications

- **For dealer onboarding scripts and Part 137 prep**: the Section 2209 NPRM is a defensive tailwind, not a new compliance burden. Update [content/compliance-program.md](../../content/compliance-program.md)'s Part 107/137 prep curriculum to add a one-line entry under the "airspace transit" topic: "If the FAA finalizes the proposed § 74.250 from the May 6, 2026 NPRM, Part 137 operators will need to (a) broadcast Remote ID, (b) take the shortest practicable transit, and (c) notify the facility before transiting a Standard UAFR. The rule does not yet exist; comments close July 6, 2026."
- **For Terraplex network-level advocacy**: the 60-day comment period (closes July 6, 2026) is the leverage point. If Terraplex wants to file a comment defending the Part 137 transit allowance — which is structurally favorable to the [49-location dealer network](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) — that needs to be queued in the Sam Welton compliance program work plan. The simplest unified position is "preserve § 74.250 as drafted; do not narrow Part 137 access in response to fixed-site-facility comments."
- **For positioning copy and country-of-origin claims**: the Anzu/Drone Nerds escalation specifically validates "headquartered and operated within the United States" as a category of distributor claim that state AGs are willing to litigate. Audit [content/positioning.md](../../content/positioning.md)'s "Built in the Americas, for the American Farmer" language and the [Terraplex profile](../manufacturers/terraplex.md)'s manufacturer-relationship description against the substantiation bar Paxton is establishing: every provenance claim should be paired with a documentable supply-chain fact (assembly location, primary component sourcing, OEM relationship). This is a copy-audit task, not a fix-now task — Terraplex's claims are factually grounded — but the bar moved.
- **For dealer competitive-tracking**: the Anzu story is mostly *not* an ag-drone story — Anzu Raptor is a small enterprise/inspection drone, not a spray drone. It enters the dealer competitive grid only as a "what about Anzu?" prospect question that should be answered "Anzu is the subject of an ongoing Texas state enforcement action over alleged misrepresentation of country-of-origin on a DJI-Mavic-3-Enterprise rebrand line; it is not a serious ag-spray platform and is not a peer of Terraplex's GTEEX-distributed lineup."
- **For the regulatory roundup file**: the [April 2026 regulatory roundup](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md) should be supplemented (not rewritten — the regulatory file's revision convention is append-only) at the next monthly intelligence pass with a Section 2209 NPRM entry under "FAA UAS rulemaking in process," documenting Document 2026-08943 / Docket FAA-2026-4558 / Notice 26-03 and the July 6 comment deadline.
