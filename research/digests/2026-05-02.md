## Summary
The American Spray Drone Coalition's 2025 industry survey reports US ag-drone treated acreage rose 58.7% year-over-year to 16.4 million acres while new unit sales fell 59% (8,950 in 2024 to 3,711 in 2025) — demand grew sharply even as DJI import constraints tightened available supply. Separately, fixed-wing autonomous-aircraft maker Pyka closed deals on two fronts: an expanded fleet rollout with Brazilian ag giant SLC Agricola (target of 30 additional Pelican 2 deliveries by end of 2026 against 7 delivered through 2025) and confirmation of Heinen Brothers' Kelly Hills Unmanned Systems as the first US customer of the 300-liter Pelican 2 — the largest UAS the FAA has authorized for commercial agricultural use.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers
The Coalition data quantifies what dealers feel anecdotally: customers want more drone-applied acres than the channel can currently deliver new airframes for. With unit sales down 59% in a market with +58.7% acreage growth, used-airframe pricing, parts availability, and the lifetime value of a service-tier customer relationship all tilt in the dealer's favor through 2026. This is also the data point to anchor "the market is real and growing" against any customer who is sitting on the fence waiting for the FCC/DJI dust to settle.

Pyka's Brazilian and US momentum is a different competitive vector: fixed-wing autonomous aircraft (300L payload, ~100 hectares per hour ≈ 247 ac/hr, night-capable) operate at a throughput tier no multirotor matches and at a price point that competes with piloted aircraft and ground rigs, not with the R-32 or I-19. Dealers should expect a "what about Pyka?" question from prospects evaluating biologics-at-night, large-acreage row-crop spray, or aerial-applicator alternatives — and the right answer is that Pelican 2 is a different category of asset (capex, runway/road requirements, certification overhead, swath behavior) rather than a head-to-head substitute. Notably, the US Pyka customer is a vertically integrated ag retailer subsidiary (Heinen Brothers' Kelly Hills), which signals that the autonomous-fixed-wing channel is being built through retailer-distribution relationships much like Terraplex's own model — relevant context for how dealers describe their position to customers comparing options.

## Key facts

### Item 1: 2025 US ag-drone market: +58.7% acreage growth, −59% new-unit sales
- The American Spray Drone Coalition's annual industry survey reports total US ag-drone-treated acreage surged 58.7% year-over-year to 16.4 million acres in 2025 [source: https://agfundernews.com/dji-faces-uncertain-future-in-us-as-ag-spray-drone-industry-readies-for-realignment]
- New spray drone unit sales fell 59% in the same period: from approximately 8,950 units sold in 2024 to approximately 3,711 in 2025, attributed to US Customs and Border Protection import restrictions on DJI shipments (context: FCC covered-list ruling occurred 2025-12-22; see [April 2026 regulatory roundup](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md) and [2026-05-01 digest](2026-05-01.md) for backdrop) [source: https://agfundernews.com/dji-faces-uncertain-future-in-us-as-ag-spray-drone-industry-readies-for-realignment]
- US-made-drone market share roughly quadrupled in 2025; the survey notes the gain "primarily reflects the vacuum left by the sudden drop in DJI imports rather than a sustained competitive shift," with XAG and EAVision picking up the most reallocated demand alongside domestic players [source: https://agfundernews.com/dji-faces-uncertain-future-in-us-as-ag-spray-drone-industry-readies-for-realignment]
- Domestic manufacturers cited as picking up new business include Texas-based [Hylio](../manufacturers/hylio.md) and Vermont-based Ceres Air, which is beginning US manufacturing of drones [source: https://agfundernews.com/dji-faces-uncertain-future-in-us-as-ag-spray-drone-industry-readies-for-realignment]
- Separately, [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) released its own global usage report stating DJI ag drones now spray "300 crops in 100 countries," with 400,000 agricultural drones (spray and crop-scouting combined) operating worldwide in 2024 — a 33% year-over-year increase [source: https://agfundernews.com/dji-report-charts-rapid-rise-in-global-adoption-of-ag-spray-drones]
- DJI's report adds that drones globally have treated more than 1.2 billion acres (≈500 million hectares) of farmland as of June 2024, with 6,000 instructors and 300,000 pilots trained [source: https://www.agweb.com/news/machinery/machinery-tech-news-more-john-deere-layoffs-dji-details-global-ag-drone-usage]

### Item 2: Pyka momentum — SLC Agricola fleet expansion in Brazil; Kelly Hills as first US customer
- Pyka has expanded its agreement with SLC Agricola, a leading Brazilian agribusiness producing soybeans, corn, cotton, and cattle, after SLC tested the Pelican 2 in the prior growing season; SLC is now scaling its fleet "to continue driving innovation and productivity across our operations" [source: https://agfundernews.com/brazilian-ag-giant-slc-agricola-scales-use-of-unmanned-crop-sprayers-with-pyka]
- Pyka delivery cadence: 7 Pelican 2 aircraft delivered by end of 2025; target of 30 additional deliveries by end of 2026, with the next two years focused on Brazil, Central American banana-producing countries, and the US [source: https://agfundernews.com/brazilian-ag-giant-slc-agricola-scales-use-of-unmanned-crop-sprayers-with-pyka]
- Pelican 2 specs: 300-liter payload, fully autonomous flight, work rate up to 100 hectares per hour (≈247 acres/hour); described as the largest autonomous agricultural aircraft in the world and the only UAS at this scale authorized by the FAA for commercial operation [source: https://agfundernews.com/brazilian-ag-giant-slc-agricola-scales-use-of-unmanned-crop-sprayers-with-pyka]
- Pyka CEO Michael Norcia on the Brazil opportunity: "The ability to spray at night is probably the single largest value proposition" — biologic pesticides popular in Brazil degrade in heat and must be applied after dark [source: https://agfundernews.com/brazilian-ag-giant-slc-agricola-scales-use-of-unmanned-crop-sprayers-with-pyka]
- US channel: Kelly Hills Unmanned Systems, a subsidiary of Kansas-based ag retailer Heinen Brothers Agra Services established in 2024, debuted as the first US customer for Pyka's Pelican Spray and is now integrating Pelican 2 (300L capacity, up to 222 acres per hour at 60-foot swath) into its aerial-application service fleet [source: https://agfundernews.com/kelly-hills-unmanned-systems-debuts-with-the-largest-commercial-ag-drone-in-the-us]
- Pelican Spray was billed by Pyka as the largest commercially available UAS in the US at debut; the platform is electric-powered with GPS and LiDAR for autonomous takeoff, flight, and landing, and was developed over seven years out of California [source: https://www.agweb.com/news/business/technology/first-look-kelly-hills-unmanned-unveils-massive-made-usa-spray-drone]
- Pyka's own framing on positioning: total cost of application is lower than a piloted aircraft and "quite similar to a boom sprayer when accounting for crop yield loss" — competes against piloted aircraft and ground rigs on price, not against multirotor airframes [source: https://agfundernews.com/brazilian-ag-giant-slc-agricola-scales-use-of-unmanned-crop-sprayers-with-pyka]

## Implications
- **For dealer positioning copy**: anchor "the ag-drone market is growing fast" claims to the Coalition's 16.4 million acres (+58.7% YoY) figure — it is the most defensible single market-size data point currently available and cites a third-party industry survey rather than a vendor source. Pair it with the −59% unit-sales drop only when the audience is a sophisticated buyer who can hold both facts; for general dealer marketing copy, the acreage growth alone tells the story without inviting the "but supply is down" follow-up.
- **For pricing conversations**: the supply-constrained 2026 environment supports holding MAP firm — there is no commercial reason to discount when serviceable inventory clears at list. Customers asking "should I wait for prices to fall?" should be redirected to the underlying data: 4× year-over-year growth in domestic-manufactured market share means more buyers chasing fewer airframes, not fewer.
- **For competitive objection handling**: Pyka Pelican 2 is not a head-to-head substitute for an R-32 or I-19. The honest comparison frame is: (a) Pyka competes with piloted aircraft and ground rigs at the 1,000+ acre/day operational scale; (b) it requires runway access (or compliant takeoff infrastructure), pilot certification overhead, and a capex tier that puts it outside the typical multirotor buyer's evaluation set; (c) for biologics-at-night and very-large-row-crop spray, it may be the right tool — but those are aerial-applicator workflows, not the dealer-channel customer profile. Dealers should not pretend the platform doesn't exist; they should explain why their multirotor offering targets a different operator and operational tempo.
- **For onboarding scripts**: add a "what about fixed-wing autonomous like Pyka?" objection handler distinct from the existing DJI/Hylio comparison — the operational model (runway, single high-throughput aircraft vs. swarm/multirotor flexibility, capex tier) is the differentiator, not raw spec sheets.
- **For territory and channel thinking**: Heinen Brothers building Kelly Hills as a Pyka-distribution subsidiary mirrors the ag-retailer-as-distributor model and validates the broader move to consolidate aerial-application via integrated retailers — context worth tracking against [Terraplex's own 49-location dealer network](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) and existing [competitor distributor profiles](../dealer-economics/competitor-distributors.md).
