## Summary
On May 6, 2026 the FAA published its long-awaited Section 2209 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in the Federal Register (Document 2026-08943, Docket FAA-2026-4558), proposing a new 14 CFR Part 74 that establishes a petition-based framework letting operators of "fixed-site facilities" apply for designated Unmanned Aircraft Flight Restrictions (UAFRs) over their airspace. The rule defines two tiers — a Standard UAFR (most drone operations prohibited unless prior FAA safety/security requirements are met) and a Special UAFR (operations prohibited unless the operator receives advance approval from both the FAA and the sponsoring federal agency) — and limits eligibility to facilities meeting the federal definition of critical infrastructure under 42 U.S.C. 5195c(e), drawing from the 16 sectors named in National Security Memorandum 22 of April 30, 2024, including a "food and agriculture" sector. Critically for ag-spray operators, the rule preserves a transit carve-out at proposed § 74.250: operators flying under Parts 91, 107, 108, 135, and **137** retain the ability to transit Standard UAFR airspace provided they broadcast Remote ID under Part 89, transit in the shortest practicable time, and notify the facility in advance. Public comments are due **July 6, 2026**, ending a 60-day comment period.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers
This is the most consequential standing-rule change for ag-spray operators since the FCC ruling captured in the [April regulatory roundup](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md), and it lands during the same Part 137 onboarding cycle that the dealer compliance program (see [content/compliance-program.md](../../content/compliance-program.md)) is built around. Two things make it dealer-actionable rather than abstract regulatory news. First, the Part 137 transit carve-out at § 74.250 is preserved — meaning a contracted ag-spray mission near a UAFR-protected site can still legally transit, but the operator must (a) broadcast Remote ID, (b) take the shortest practicable path through the restricted airspace, and (c) notify the facility in advance. That converts a "stay 5 miles clear of the ethanol plant" rule-of-thumb into a documented pre-flight workflow that dealers will need to teach the operator-customer to execute correctly. Second, "food and agriculture" is one of the 16 critical-infrastructure sectors NSM-22 names, which means the very facilities ag-spray missions tend to operate near — grain elevators, ethanol plants, food-processing plants, fertilizer distribution sites, large livestock operations — could themselves petition the FAA to become UAFR-protected over the next 12 months. The dealer's customer is structurally on both sides of this rule: as a potential petitioner (a co-op grain elevator wanting protection) and as a potential transit-affected operator (a Part 137 mission flying past it).

The 60-day comment window (closing July 6, 2026) is also a real opportunity rather than performative. AUVSI, the Commercial Drone Alliance, and the Drone Service Providers Alliance have already telegraphed comments emphasizing that "notification requirements scale with commercial operations rather than impede them" — a fight Terraplex dealers and their operator-customers have a direct interest in joining, particularly on the question of how "advance notification" will work in practice for a Part 137 mission that may file an operations plan the morning of the spray window. Dealers willing to file substantive comments (or coordinate operator-customer comments through the [49-location dealer network](../manufacturers/terraplex.md)) can influence the operational shape of the final rule.

## Key facts

### FAA Section 2209 NPRM published May 6, 2026 — proposes new 14 CFR Part 74 with Part 137 transit carve-out
- The NPRM was published in the Federal Register on May 6, 2026 as Document 2026-08943, "Designation — Restrict the Operation of Unmanned Aircraft in Close Proximity to a Fixed Site Facility," opening a 60-day public comment period that closes **July 6, 2026** under docket **FAA-2026-4558** [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility]
- The rule implements Section 2209 of the FAA Extension, Safety, and Security Act of 2016 (PUBLIC LAW 114-190), nearly a decade after Congress directed the FAA to create a process for fixed-site facility operators to petition for restricted airspace [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/06/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-drone-restrictions-around-critical-infrastructure/]
- The proposed rule is 181 pages and creates a new 14 CFR Part 74 establishing a petition-based framework under which eligible facility operators apply to the FAA for a designated **Unmanned Aircraft Flight Restriction (UAFR)** over their airspace [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/06/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-drone-restrictions-around-critical-infrastructure/]
- **Two-tier framework**: a **Standard UAFR** prohibits most drone operations within a defined boundary unless the operator previously met FAA safety and security requirements; a **Special UAFR** imposes stricter controls, with operations prohibited unless the operator receives advance approval from both the FAA and the sponsoring federal agency [source: https://www.commercialuavnews.com/faa-nprm-drone-flight-restrictions-critical-infrastructure] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/06/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-drone-restrictions-around-critical-infrastructure/]
- **Eligibility**: limited to facilities meeting the federal definition of critical infrastructure under 42 U.S.C. 5195c(e) and falling within one of the 16 critical infrastructure sectors named in National Security Memorandum 22 of April 30, 2024. Those sectors include chemical, commercial facilities, communications, critical manufacturing, dams, defense industrial base, emergency services, energy, financial services, **food and agriculture**, government services and facilities, healthcare and public health, information technology, nuclear reactors / materials / waste, transportation systems, and water and wastewater [source: https://www.commercialuavnews.com/faa-nprm-drone-flight-restrictions-critical-infrastructure] [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility]
- **Transit carve-out at proposed § 74.250**: operators conducting work under Parts 91, 107, 108, 135, and **137** retain access to Standard UAFR airspace, provided they (a) broadcast Remote ID under Part 89, (b) transit in the shortest practicable time, and (c) notify the facility in advance. Special UAFR airspace does not get this carve-out and requires advance approval [source: https://www.commercialuavnews.com/faa-nprm-drone-flight-restrictions-critical-infrastructure] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/06/faa-publishes-proposed-rule-for-drone-restrictions-around-critical-infrastructure/]
- **Industry response**: AUVSI's published statement says it "prioritizes ensuring certificated operators, including under the forthcoming Part 108, retain meaningful access to UAFR airspace, and that notification requirements scale with commercial operations rather than impede them"; AUVSI lists infrastructure inspection, agriculture, and commercial services as the access priorities for its forthcoming comment [source: https://www.auvsi.org/news/auvsi-statement-on-faas-proposed-section-2209-rule]
- **Comment submission**: comments may be filed via the Federal eRulemaking Portal at https://www.regulations.gov/ identified by docket number FAA-2026-4558 [source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/06/2026-08943/designation-restrict-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-in-close-proximity-to-a-fixed-site-facility]
- **Standing-context**: builds on, but does not duplicate, the FAA Drone Expedited and Targeted Enforcement Response (DETER) Program (effective 2026-04-17, captured in the [2026-05-04 digest](2026-05-04.md)) — DETER governs how the FAA enforces violations after they occur; Section 2209 / Part 74 governs which airspace those violations exist in to begin with

## Implications

- **For dealer onboarding and Part 137 prep**: update the [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md) and Sam Welton's Part 107 prep curriculum to teach a concrete pre-flight workflow for transiting a Standard UAFR — Remote ID broadcast confirmation, shortest-practicable routing as a flight-plan requirement (not a heuristic), and the advance-notification step. Operators who treat "fly around it" as the default plan will be fine; operators who need to fly *through* a UAFR (most common use case: a long-section field directly adjacent to or split by a UAFR-protected facility) need a documented procedure or they will violate the rule. None of this exists in the current curriculum because Part 74 didn't exist yet.
- **For dealer-customer conversations**: lead with the carve-out, not the restriction. The headline ag-press framing of this NPRM will be "FAA restricts drones around critical infrastructure" — which prospects will interpret as "I won't be able to spray near the grain elevator." The accurate dealer answer is: Part 137 keeps transit access, but transit is now a documented step instead of an assumption. Reassuring on access *and* honest about workflow change.
- **For positioning copy**: do **not** rewrite "Part 137-ready" claims yet — the rule is an NPRM, not a final rule, and the comment window runs to July 6, 2026. Premature copy that bakes in the proposed § 74.250 transit framework would need to be re-edited if comments shift the carve-out structure (e.g., notification timing requirements). Wait for the final rule.
- **For dealer territory thinking**: dealers serving territories with high concentrations of ethanol plants, grain-elevator co-ops, or food-processing facilities (Iowa, Nebraska, Kansas, Illinois) face the highest UAFR-petition density risk — those facilities are exactly the sponsors most likely to apply once the rule is final. Dealers in those territories should track which co-ops and processors file petitions and brief their operator-customers preemptively rather than wait for a UAFR to appear on a sectional chart.
- **For dealer advocacy**: the comment period (closing July 6, 2026) is a real lever. A dealer or operator filing a substantive comment on, e.g., how "advance notification" should accommodate same-morning Part 137 operations plans, has measurable influence on the final rule. The [49-location dealer network](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) can coordinate a multi-dealer comment that carries more weight than individual filings — particularly on the operational-realism questions the NPRM's authors are unlikely to have solved without ag-industry input.
