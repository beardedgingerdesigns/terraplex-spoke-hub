## Summary

The FCC's Office of Engineering and Technology released **Public Notice DA 26-454 on Friday, May 8, 2026**, extending the existing waiver that permits previously authorized foreign-made UAS (including DJI Agras platforms), UAS critical components, and consumer-grade routers to continue receiving software and firmware updates — until **at least January 1, 2029**. The order moves the firmware-support cliff out by two years (the prior waiver under DA 26-12 expired January 1, 2027) and **broadens scope to include Class II permissive changes** — substantive software and firmware modifications that mitigate consumer harm — in addition to the Class I changes (security patches, bug fixes, OS compatibility) the prior waiver already permitted. Eligibility is tied to pre-Covered-List authorization: drones authorized on or before December 22, 2025, and consumer routers authorized on or before March 23, 2026. The waiver explicitly does **not** modify Covered List status, does **not** reopen new equipment authorizations for DJI, Autel, or TP-Link, and does **not** affect Blue UAS or federal-procurement requirements. The rationale recorded in the order is that cutting off security patches for the millions of already-deployed Covered-List devices would create a worse cybersecurity outcome than the underlying ban was designed to prevent.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers

This is the **single most material change** to the existing-DJI-fleet operating environment since the December 22, 2025 Covered List ruling itself, and it shifts the dealer-customer conversation in a specific direction that the prior digests could not anticipate. The [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md) characterized the operational consequence of Commissioner Trusty's May 7 SCSP keynote as "your existing Agras fleet is the fleet you'll be operating for several years" — a conclusion built on the assumption that firmware/software support would persist through the prior January 1, 2027 cliff. DA 26-454 extends that horizon to **January 1, 2029** and — crucially — adds Class II changes to the permissive-change scope, which means dealers can now tell customers that the firmware lifeline is not just security-patch-only but can include substantive functional fixes that "mitigate consumer harm." That is two additional full spray seasons (2027 and 2028) of cybersecurity-supported operation on already-authorized Agras airframes, which is the timeframe over which most installed-base T30 / T40 / T50 platforms will see normal end-of-economic-life decisions anyway. The operational planning question is no longer "do we need to harvest the existing fleet before the firmware cuts off?" — it is "what is the natural replacement cycle, and what is the right cross-grade path when individual units retire?"

Two caveats temper the read. **First**, the waiver does not in any way change the Covered List itself or open the door to new DJI Agras SKUs entering the US market — the unreleased Agras T55 referenced in trade-press coverage is still blocked from authorization, and the [DJI Agras profile's](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) revision-2026-05-01 finding that "the constraint is on new SKUs and re-authorizations, not on operation of already-authorized airframes" remains the operative framing. **Second**, this is an OET Public Notice (DA-level action, delegated authority) rather than a full Commission ruling; it sits alongside, but is procedurally distinct from, the still-pending DJI Petition for Reconsideration (ET Docket 26-22) and Autel Application for Review (ET Docket 26-23) whose reply-comment window closed end-of-day Monday, May 11. There is a subtle internal-agency narrative tension visible across May 7–May 11: Commissioner Trusty publicly defended the Covered List rationale on May 7 ([2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md)), while OET on May 8 issued a delegated-authority order that concedes a full firmware cutoff would do more cybersecurity harm than good. Both can be true simultaneously — the agency can hold the line on new-equipment authorizations while pragmatically extending firmware support for the installed base — but dealers should not over-read DA 26-454 as a softening signal on the underlying ban. It is not.

## Key facts

### FCC OET Public Notice DA 26-454 extends foreign-made drone & router firmware waiver to January 1, 2029 and broadens scope to Class II permissive changes

- The FCC's **Office of Engineering and Technology released Public Notice DA 26-454 on Friday, May 8, 2026** [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf] [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/]
- **New expiration**: the order grants waivers permitting software and firmware updates for previously authorized Covered UAS, UAS critical components, and consumer-grade routers **until at least January 1, 2029** — extending the prior waiver (which was set to expire January 1, 2027) by two years [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf] [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/fcc-grants-two-year-software-lifeline-for-dji-drone-and-tp-link-router-owners/]
- **Statutory scope**: the order waives the prohibitions in **47 CFR §§ 2.932(b) and 2.1043(b)** as applied to software/firmware updates that mitigate harm to US consumers [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf]
- **Broadened scope — Class II permissive changes now covered**: the prior waivers permitted only **Class I permissive changes** (modifications that do not require a new FCC filing — security patches, bug fixes, OS-compatibility updates); DA 26-454 expands the scope to also cover **Class II permissive changes** (more substantial software and firmware modifications intended to mitigate consumer harm) [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/] [source: https://www.notebookcheck.net/TP-Link-DJI-and-other-foreign-made-devices-get-FCC-update-waiver.1293540.0.html]
- **Eligibility cutoffs are unchanged from prior waiver**: UAS / UAS critical components must have been **authorized prior to the December 22, 2025 Covered List addition**; consumer-grade routers must have been **authorized prior to the March 23, 2026 Covered List addition**. Equipment authorized after those dates is not eligible [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf]
- **Manufacturers explicitly covered**: DJI (including the Agras agricultural line), Autel Robotics, and TP-Link [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/] [source: https://www.suasnews.com/2026/05/fcc-grants-two-year-software-lifeline-for-dji-drone-and-tp-link-router-owners/]
- **DJI Agras T55 — unreleased model — remains blocked**: trade-press coverage explicitly notes the unreleased T55 (which would have been the 2026 flagship) cannot benefit from this waiver and remains blocked from US authorization under the Covered List ruling; the waiver applies to already-authorized airframes only [source: https://uavcoach.com/dji-firmware-2026/]
- **Stated rationale**: the order cites "the continued safe operation of existing models" and the concern that **blocking security patches on the millions of already-deployed DJI drones, Autel drones, and TP-Link routers in American hands would create a worse cybersecurity problem** than the Covered List ruling itself was designed to address [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/] [source: https://www.heliosvisions.com/2026/05/11/the-fcc-just-bought-the-drone-and-it-industries-three-more-years-to-get-this-right/]
- **What DA 26-454 does NOT do**: it does **not** remove any device from the Covered List; does **not** roll back the broader ban on new equipment authorizations; does **not** create any new market access for DJI, Autel, or TP-Link; does **not** affect Blue UAS or federal-procurement requirements [source: https://www.notebookcheck.net/TP-Link-DJI-and-other-foreign-made-devices-get-FCC-update-waiver.1293540.0.html] [source: https://uavcoach.com/dji-firmware-2026/]
- **Procedural posture**: DA 26-454 is a Public Notice issued under delegated authority by the OET Bureau — distinct from a full Commission vote — and sits alongside, but procedurally separate from, the still-pending **DJI Petition for Reconsideration (ET Docket 26-22)** and **Autel Application for Review (ET Docket 26-23)** whose reply-comment window closed Monday, May 11 (context: ruling occurred 2025-12-22; deadline established March 6, 2026 per DA 26-223 — see [2026-05-08 digest](2026-05-08.md)) [source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-454A1.pdf]
- **Continuity with prior orders**: DA 26-454 is the successor to **DA 26-12** (the January 2026 order that first established the firmware waiver with a January 1, 2027 expiration); it both extends the timeline and broadens the permissive-change scope (Class I → Class I + Class II) — see the [DJI Agras profile](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) revision-2026-05-01 baseline for the underlying installed-base economics [source: https://dronexl.co/2026/05/11/fcc-extends-foreign-drone-firmware-waiver-2029-da-26-454/]

## Implications

- **For dealer-customer conversations about existing DJI Agras fleets**: the operating-life answer is now meaningfully more durable. Where the prior framing was "you have firmware support through the 2026 spray season under DA 26-12," the updated framing is "you have firmware support through at least the 2028 spray season under DA 26-454, including Class II functional updates — not just security patches." Customers running T30 / T40 / T50 platforms can plan replacement on natural-economic-life rather than regulatory-cliff timing, which is a strictly better conversation for the dealer.
- **For "Built in the Americas" positioning** around the [GTEEX Revolution lineup](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) and [Hylio](../manufacturers/hylio.md) hardware: nothing material changes. DA 26-454 is about installed-base maintenance, not new market access. The durability of domestic-OEM regulatory standing — established across the prior digests' coverage of the Covered List ruling and the Trusty speech — is unchanged. Do **not** rewrite copy to suggest DJI is "coming back"; the order explicitly does not modify the Covered List. The honest framing remains: "your existing fleet is supported for longer than we thought; your next fleet expansion should still default to the domestic-OEM lineup because that's where new-equipment authorization actually exists."
- **For onboarding scripts** when prospects ask "will my DJI drones still get firmware updates?": the accurate post-May-8 answer is "yes, under FCC Public Notice DA 26-454 released May 8, 2026, the FCC's Office of Engineering and Technology extended the firmware-update waiver through at least January 1, 2029, and expanded it to cover not just security patches but substantive functional updates. The waiver applies to DJI Agras platforms that were FCC-authorized before December 22, 2025 — which is the platforms currently in the US installed base. The waiver does not change the Covered List or open the door to new DJI SKUs."
- **For Sam Welton's [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md) curriculum**: the post-May-11 module hook identified in the [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md) ("what Trusty's speech implies about the post-petition operating environment") should now also cover DA 26-454 as the counterpart story — same week, same agency, but a pragmatic delegated-authority concession on the installed-base maintenance question. Prep-course students need to hold both facts at the same time: the Commission is not walking back the ban, *and* the Bureau is extending firmware support because cutting it off would be worse than the ban. The teaching point is that these are not contradictions — they are the two sides of an established regulatory pattern (hard line on new authorizations, pragmatic accommodation on existing installed base).
- **For territory-level outreach** to dealers in DJI-heavy territories (Iowa, Nebraska, Kansas, Missouri, Illinois — see the [competitor distributors profile](../dealer-economics/competitor-distributors.md)): the immediate conversation shifts from "we need to plan for fleet turnover before the firmware cutoff" to "we have time to plan turnover on natural-life economics through 2028, but new-fleet quotes should still be domestic-OEM because that is where new-equipment supply will live for the foreseeable future." The Trusty-speech-driven framing from [2026-05-11](2026-05-11.md) ("the FCC's signal-from-the-top is 'the Covered List frame is the operating environment we are building on, not the one we are walking back'") is unchanged by DA 26-454 — the waiver is a maintenance accommodation, not a market-access reopening.
- **For monitoring follow-up**: watch for (1) any Commission-level vote on the still-pending DJI Petition for Reconsideration (ET Docket 26-22) and Autel Application for Review (ET Docket 26-23) — reply-comment window closed May 11, no Commission vote date set yet; (2) any DoD/DHS-issued Conditional Approval determinations that could create narrow new-equipment exceptions (Wiley Rein advisories have flagged "first Conditional Approvals" as a discrete near-term watch-item); (3) any further OET delegated-authority orders refining the DA 26-454 scope. The [2026-04 regulatory update](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md) is the right structural anchor to extend when the Commission acts on the reconsideration petition.
