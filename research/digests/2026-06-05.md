## Summary

Two parallel developments since the last digest (2026-05-16) reshape the dealer-conversation landscape entering peak 2026 spray season. **First (June 4, 2026)**: the National Agricultural Aviation Association (**NAAA**) joined six other ag-aviation associations across the Americas — Argentina's **FeArCA**, Brazil's **Sindag**, Uruguay's **Anepa**, the **Mexican Federation of the Agricultural Aviation Industry**, the **Agricultural Aviation Association of Paraguay**, and Canada's **CAAA** — in a joint letter to regulators across all seven countries calling for "clear, modern, comprehensive, and effectively enforceable regulatory frameworks" governing drone operations in agricultural airspace. The letter follows NAAA's **April 20, 2026** annual season-opening safety advisory citing that **20% of US manned aerial-application operators reported at least one unsafe encounter with a drone in 2025** (up from 16% in 2024 and 11% in 2023). **Second (May 28, 2026)**: DJI released the findings of an independent security assessment conducted by US-based cybersecurity firm **OnDefend** (engagement October 2025 through March 2026) covering the **DJI Air 3S** (with RC 2 controller) and **DJI Matrice 4E** (with RC Plus 2 Enterprise controller) — producing **zero critical, high, or medium-risk findings** across data-sovereignty, hardware-vulnerability, and drone-manipulation-risk testing domains. The OnDefend report is DJI's most substantive public defense yet of its FCC Covered List position (context: FCC ruling 2025-12-22; DJI Petition for Reconsideration ET Docket 26-22 still pending decision per [2026-05-13 digest](2026-05-13.md)); the [DJI Agras Ninth Circuit appeal](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) (Case 26-1029) remains the parallel structural track.

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers

These items sit on **opposite sides** of the dealer-conversation map: the NAAA letter is operational/safety-facing (what your operator does in the field, every flight); the DJI/OnDefend assessment is regulatory/positioning-facing (how your customer thinks about the "is DJI a real security risk?" question that's now nine months old). Both belong in dealer-readiness for the 2026 spray season because both reframe questions customers are actively asking right now.

**On the NAAA front**, the 20% unsafe-encounter statistic (up from 11% in 2023 — a ~doubling in two years) is the most concrete data point yet that ag-drone-vs-manned-ag-aircraft conflict has become a measurable system-wide problem rather than an anecdote. The June 4 multi-national letter elevates this from a single-country trade-group advisory to a coordinated regulatory-pressure campaign across the Americas. For Terraplex dealers selling to operators who will share airspace with crop-dusters this season, the practical takeaways are unchanged but now louder: drones legally yield right-of-way to manned aircraft (FAA regulation, not a courtesy); equip with visible strobe lights and high-visibility markings; monitor **aviation radio frequency 122.925 MHz** for nearby crewed agricultural aircraft; land immediately when a low-flying manned aircraft enters the area. The onboarding hand-off scripts and pre-season briefings should now reference the 20% statistic directly — both because it's a credible safety case and because it pre-empts the "drone operators are reckless cowboys" framing the NAAA campaign is feeding to state regulators. Dealers who position their operators as safety-disciplined (radio-monitoring, ADS-B In equipped, visible markings, immediate-land discipline) are on the right side of the regulatory pressure curve; dealers who don't surface this in operator training are taking on regulatory and reputational risk that's now actively being documented by NAAA.

**On the DJI/OnDefend front**, the dealer-conversation question is no longer "what does the FCC say?" — that's settled per the [2026-05-13 digest's](2026-05-13.md) DA 26-454 firmware-waiver extension to at least January 1, 2029 (Covered List intact, installed-base patches permitted through 2028). The new live question is "what does the technical evidence actually show, and does that change anything?" The honest dealer answer post-May-28 is **nuanced**: an independent US-based security firm (OnDefend) with no DJI commercial relationship tested the consumer-prosumer Air 3S and enterprise Matrice 4E platforms and found zero issues — no hidden backdoors, no out-of-country data transmissions, no hijacking pathways. **But** the FCC's December 22, 2025 covered-list designation is supported by DoD classified intelligence (per the 2026-04-10 Pentagon memo cited in the [2026-05-09 digest](2026-05-09.md) and reaffirmed in FCC Commissioner Trusty's May 7 SCSP AI+ Expo keynote per the [2026-05-11 digest](2026-05-11.md)) — and that intelligence is not addressed by any published security audit. The dealer should not over-claim the OnDefend report as exoneration; it tests **what a security firm can see**, and the federal architecture asserts the actual risk is **what only classified channels can see**. The OnDefend test also did not cover the [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) ag-spray platforms specifically — Air 3S and Matrice 4E are aerial-imaging platforms, not ag-spray UAVs — so even on its own terms the audit doesn't speak directly to the T30/T40/T50 fleet currently in dealer customers' hands. The "Built in the Americas" structural pitch from the [2026-05-14 digest](2026-05-14.md) (Cadenazzi Section 805 timeline, FAA "drones as aircraft" framing, AUVSI consensus) is not weakened by this report; the dealer talking point becomes "DJI's security defense is now substantive on the parts of its lineup that have been independently audited, but the next-fleet decision still hinges on Section 805 enforcement starting June 30, 2026 and the FCC ruling on the Petition for Reconsideration, both of which the OnDefend audit does not affect."

## Key facts

### NAAA joint multi-national letter on drone regulatory frameworks (June 4, 2026)

- **Seven signatory organizations**: NAAA (United States), CAAA (Canada — Canadian Aerial Applicators Association), FeArCA (Argentina — Federación Argentina de Cámaras de Aviación Agrícola), Sindag (Brazil — Sindicato Nacional das Empresas de Aviação Agrícola), Anepa (Uruguay — Asociación Nacional de Empresas Privadas de Aviación Agrícola), Mexican Federation of the Agricultural Aviation Industry, Agricultural Aviation Association of Paraguay — collectively branded **Agricultural Aviation Organizations of the Americas** [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/04/naaa-joins-agricultural-aviation-organizations-of-the-americas-with-a-letter-calling-for-modern-safe-drone-regulatory-frameworks/]
- **Letter content**: expresses "deep concerns regarding the growing use of drones" and calls for "the implementation of clear, modern, comprehensive, and effectively enforceable regulatory frameworks" governing drone operations across each signatory country — addressed to regulatory agencies in all seven nations [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/04/naaa-joins-agricultural-aviation-organizations-of-the-americas-with-a-letter-calling-for-modern-safe-drone-regulatory-frameworks/]
- **Stated rationale in letter**: manned agricultural aircraft fly at low altitudes and high speeds (10 ft above ground, up to 140 mph) making it difficult or impossible to see and avoid drones during spraying operations [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/04/naaa-joins-agricultural-aviation-organizations-of-the-americas-with-a-letter-calling-for-modern-safe-drone-regulatory-frameworks/]

### NAAA 2025-season unsafe-encounter statistic (April 20, 2026 advisory, reiterated in June 4 letter context)

- **20% of US manned aerial-application operators reported at least one unsafe interaction with a drone during 2025** — up from 16% in 2024 and 11% in 2023 (i.e., roughly a doubling in two years) [source: https://www.brownfieldagnews.com/news/ag-drone-pilots-urged-to-yield-to-manned-aircraft/] [source: https://www.americanagnetwork.com/2026/04/20/ag-aviation-industry-urges-drone-operators-to-give-right-of-way-to-manned-agricultural-aircraft-this-growing-season/] [source: https://avweb.com/flight-safety/ag-aircraft-drone-encounters-increase-2025/]
- **NAAA April 20, 2026 season-opening advisory** to drone operators (context: published prior to the prior digest cadence and not previously captured): drones must yield right-of-way to manned aircraft as a matter of law (FAA regulation, not industry preference) [source: https://www.brownfieldagnews.com/news/ag-drone-pilots-urged-to-yield-to-manned-aircraft/] [source: https://www.americanagnetwork.com/2026/04/20/ag-aviation-industry-urges-drone-operators-to-give-right-of-way-to-manned-agricultural-aircraft-this-growing-season/]
- **NAAA-recommended drone operator practices** for the 2026 season: equip drones with **visible strobe lights** and **highly visible markings**; install **ADS-B In** tracking technology; **monitor aviation radio frequency 122.925 MHz** for crewed agricultural aircraft (the standard ag-aviation safety frequency, also referenced in AgAir Update's [June 1, 2026 "Fly Safe" piece](https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/01/fly-safe-managing-risk-in-the-2026-ag-aviation-season/) on manned ag-aviation risk management); **land the unmanned aircraft immediately** when a low-flying manned aircraft is nearby — described by NAAA spokesperson Williams as "by far the safest approach" [source: https://www.brownfieldagnews.com/news/ag-drone-pilots-urged-to-yield-to-manned-aircraft/] [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/01/fly-safe-managing-risk-in-the-2026-ag-aviation-season/]
- **Regulatory ceiling note**: UAS are not permitted to operate above 400 feet without an FAA waiver, meaning ag drones and manned ag aircraft are necessarily sharing the same sub-400-foot low-altitude band during spray operations — the geometric basis for the conflict [source: https://www.americanagnetwork.com/2026/04/20/ag-aviation-industry-urges-drone-operators-to-give-right-of-way-to-manned-agricultural-aircraft-this-growing-season/]

### DJI OnDefend independent security assessment (released May 28, 2026)

- **Auditor**: **OnDefend**, US-based cybersecurity firm; engagement ran **October 2025 through March 2026** [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment] [source: https://petapixel.com/2026/05/28/independent-audit-finds-no-security-basis-for-restricting-dji-in-the-usa/] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/28/dji-releases-independent-security-assessment-as-fcc-covered-list-debate-continues/]
- **Scope — platforms tested**: **DJI Air 3S** (with RC 2 controller) and **DJI Matrice 4E** (with RC Plus 2 Enterprise controller) — i.e., consumer-prosumer aerial-imaging + enterprise platforms; the [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) T30/T40/T50 ag-spray series was **not** within the audited scope [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment] [source: https://dronedj.com/2026/05/28/dji-drone-ban-security-report/]
- **Scope — threat domains tested**: structured around the three national-security concerns underlying the FCC Covered List rationale: (1) **data sovereignty** (do drone systems transmit data outside the United States?); (2) **hardware vulnerabilities** (are there hidden backdoors at the silicon or firmware layer?); (3) **drone manipulation risks** (can the systems be hijacked, jammed, or weaponized via RF or software pathways?). Testing covered software, hardware, and radio-frequency domains with "advanced adversarial testing" methodology [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment] [source: https://petapixel.com/2026/05/28/independent-audit-finds-no-security-basis-for-restricting-dji-in-the-usa/]
- **Findings**: **zero critical, high, or medium-risk findings**. Specifically: "no clear evidence of hidden backdoors, no data transmissions outside the United States, and no viable pathways for hijacking or weaponization" [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment] [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/28/dji-releases-independent-security-assessment-as-fcc-covered-list-debate-continues/]
- **DJI on-record framing**: "These findings confirm what DJI has consistently maintained: our products are secure, our data practices are transparent, and the concerns underlying our FCC Covered List designation are not supported by technical evidence." [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment]
- **FCC procedural state of play**: the OnDefend report was published into the **DJI Petition for Reconsideration window (ET Docket 26-22)**, whose reply-comment deadline closed May 11, 2026 (context: [2026-05-09 digest](2026-05-09.md)); the FCC has **not yet issued a decision** on the petition as of 2026-06-05. DoD's April 10, 2026 opposition memorandum citing classified intelligence (with a classified annex submitted to Congress on April 3) remains in the docket and is **not addressed by the OnDefend audit**, which by design tested only what an independent security firm could observe with the production hardware [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/28/dji-releases-independent-security-assessment-as-fcc-covered-list-debate-continues/]

### What the OnDefend assessment does NOT establish

- The audit **does not cover the [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) T-series ag-spray platforms** (T30, T40, T50) currently in dealer customer hands — only the Air 3S aerial-imaging and Matrice 4E enterprise platforms. Dealers cannot legitimately claim the OnDefend findings apply to ag-spray DJI fleet [source: https://www.dji.com/media-center/announcements/dji-drone-independent-security-assessment]
- The audit **does not address classified intelligence** — by definition, OnDefend operated on what was observable to a US cybersecurity firm with physical access to the hardware; the DoD's April 10 memorandum and classified annex are a parallel evidentiary track that the audit cannot rebut [source: https://dronelife.com/2026/05/28/dji-releases-independent-security-assessment-as-fcc-covered-list-debate-continues/]
- The audit **does not change the FCC Covered List**, the FCC firmware waiver (DA 26-454, extended to at least 2029 per [2026-05-13 digest](2026-05-13.md)), the Ninth Circuit appeal (Case 26-1029), or the Section 805 / 1260H supply-chain enforcement timeline (per [2026-05-14 digest](2026-05-14.md)). The regulatory architecture from prior digests remains intact

## Implications

- **For dealer 2026-spray-season operator hand-off / onboarding materials**: explicitly surface the NAAA-recommended practices as **required**, not optional — visible strobe lights, high-visibility markings, ADS-B In, **monitor 122.925 MHz**, immediate-land discipline when manned aircraft are in the area. Cite the 20% unsafe-encounter statistic in operator training to underline that this is now a measurable, escalating system-wide risk rather than an edge-case concern. Dealers whose operators are not radio-monitoring on 122.925 MHz are exposed both safety-wise and reputationally (NAAA is now actively documenting the operator behavior gap to regulators across seven countries). See [content/compliance-program.md](../../content/compliance-program.md) for the Sam Welton prep-course wrapper — radio-frequency monitoring discipline should be a Day-1 curriculum element.
- **For dealer-customer "is DJI actually a security risk?" conversations**: the post-May-28 honest answer is **two-part**. (1) "An independent US-based cybersecurity firm with no DJI commercial relationship tested DJI's consumer and enterprise aerial-imaging platforms — Air 3S and Matrice 4E — and found zero critical, high, or medium-risk findings across data-sovereignty, hardware-vulnerability, and drone-manipulation domains. The technical evidence DJI has put forward to the FCC is now substantive, not just assertion." (2) "But the FCC Covered List designation rests on classified DoD intelligence that no public security audit can address, the audit does not cover the [DJI Agras](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) ag-spray T-series specifically, and the regulatory architecture — Covered List, Section 805 enforcement starting June 30, 2026, firmware waiver through 2029 — is unchanged. The next-fleet decision still hinges on domestic OEM availability and the FCC reconsideration outcome, not on this audit." Do NOT claim the OnDefend report exonerates DJI Agras or invalidates the FCC ruling — it does neither.
- **For the [DJI Agras manufacturer profile](../manufacturers/dji-agras.md) next monthly intelligence refresh**: append a `### Revision 2026-06` entry capturing (a) the OnDefend assessment publication date, scope, and findings; (b) the explicit note that ag-spray T-series is outside scope; (c) the FCC reconsideration docket state of play remains unchanged. The DJI Agras profile's Current State paragraph does not need rewriting — the operational facts (installed-base legal, new-SKU blocked, Ninth Circuit appeal pending) are unaffected.
- **For onboarding scripts and dealer Sam-Welton-program materials referencing aviation-radio coordination**: 122.925 MHz is the canonical ag-aviation safety frequency; dealer training should include both the frequency and the listening-discipline expectation (continuous monitoring during operations, not occasional). Pair with ADS-B In capability where the dealer's hardware supports it. The 2026 spray season starts now — this is operationally time-sensitive.
- **For monitoring follow-up**: watch (a) the FCC's decision on the DJI Petition for Reconsideration (ET Docket 26-22) — no public timeline as of 2026-06-05; (b) whether the OnDefend report attracts FCC procedural treatment as a late-filed exhibit in the reconsideration docket or is addressed in any Commission order; (c) whether NAAA's June 4 multi-national letter produces any visible FAA / state-regulator response (a state-level UAS rulemaking acceleration in any of the Iowa / Texas / Nebraska / Kansas / Missouri territory states would be a meaningful signal); (d) any follow-on independent audit covering the DJI Agras ag-spray T-series specifically — that would be the more dealer-relevant data point.
