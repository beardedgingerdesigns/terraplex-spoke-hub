## Summary

On **Thursday, June 4, 2026**, the **National Agricultural Aviation Association (NAAA)** joined a six-country hemispheric coalition of agricultural-aviation trade bodies — Argentina's **FeArCA** (Federación Argentina de Cámaras Agroaéreas), Brazil's **Sindag** (Sindicato Nacional das Empresas de Aviação Agrícola), Uruguay's **Anepa** (Asociación Nacional de Empresas Privadas de Aviación Agrícola), the **Mexican Federation of the Agricultural Aviation Industry**, the **Agricultural Aviation Association of Paraguay**, and the **Canadian Aerial Applicators Association (CAAA)** — in a joint letter to civil-aviation regulators across the Americas calling for "clear, modern, comprehensive, and effectively enforceable regulatory frameworks" governing drone operations in low-altitude airspace shared with manned agricultural aircraft. The letter's six concrete asks — yield-to-manned-aircraft codification, registration/certification/mandatory insurance for spray-drone operators, drone identification and electronic-traceability systems, low-altitude coordination protocols, increased inspections, and stiffer penalties for illegal or irregular operations — track the FAA's existing Part 137 / Part 107 compliance architecture and the **NAAA 2025 industry-survey finding that 20% of US aerial-application operators reported at least one unsafe interaction with a drone during the 2025 growing season (up from 16% in 2024 and 11% in 2023)** [source: https://avweb.com/flight-safety/ag-aircraft-drone-encounters-increase-2025/]. The letter, datelined the start of the 2026 northern-hemisphere growing season, is the first multi-country agricultural-aviation policy alignment on drone safety on the public record and lands the same week as the **AgAir Update June 1, 2026 "Fly Safe: Managing Risk in the 2026 Ag Aviation Season" editorial** [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/01/fly-safe-managing-risk-in-the-2026-ag-aviation-season/], which independently calls out the same yield-to-manned-aircraft principle and recommends ferrying closer to **1,000 ft AGL** for modern turbine ag aircraft (above the legacy "ferry above five and stay alive" 500 ft AGL convention).

## Why this matters for Terraplex dealers

This is a **manned-vs-unmanned ag-aviation political alignment** event, and it lands on the dealer-facing pitch for **certificated Part 137 operations** in a way the prior six weeks of regulatory digests (FCC Covered List enforcement, Section 805 supply-chain rules, FAA Section 2209 NPRM, FAA DETER program) have not. Terraplex sells unmanned ag-spray airframes ([GTEEX Revolution](../manufacturers/terraplex.md) / I-19 / R-32) into a customer base that is — at the legitimate-operator end — already Part 137-certificated or working toward it through the Sam Welton-led [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md). The NAAA-led coalition's six asks are **substantially what a Part 137 + Part 107 dealer customer already does**: holds a Part 137 Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (registration + certification), carries Part 137 liability coverage (mandatory insurance), broadcasts Remote ID (drone identification + electronic traceability), and complies with airspace coordination protocols including the ag-traffic 122.925 MHz monitoring recommended in the NAAA right-of-way materials [source: https://avweb.com/flight-safety/ag-aircraft-drone-encounters-increase-2025/]. The dealer-positioning play is to **make the certificated/insured/Remote-ID-equipped posture explicit and central**, rather than treating it as background compliance plumbing — because the regulatory political weather is now actively asking civil-aviation authorities across seven countries to **separate the certificated-Part-137 spray-drone operator from the hobbyist/un-certificated/uninsured drone operator** that the NAAA letter is responding to.

The complementary dealer-conversation reframe is on the **rising-trend** side of the safety problem. The NAAA survey trend line (11% → 16% → 20% over 2023 → 2024 → 2025) is **going in the wrong direction**, which means the regulatory response — and likely the insurance-underwriting response — will harden over the 2026–2027 horizon regardless of whether the hemispheric letter changes any specific rule. The honest dealer message during a Part 137 prep / training / onboarding conversation is: "the certificated path is becoming a structural advantage as the rest of the drone-operator population gets more scrutiny — your Part 137 + Part 107 + Remote-ID + insurance posture is what the regulators across seven countries are now publicly asking to be the default, and being there ahead of the rule changes is worth more in 2026 than it was in 2024." This is **adjacent to but distinct from** the "Built in the Americas" / NDAA-compliance / FCC-Covered-List narrative captured across the May 2026 digests — that thread is about **what airframe you can legally buy**; this thread is about **what operator posture you can legally fly with**.

- **Recipients**: civil-aviation regulators in each signatory country (FAA in the US; Transport Canada in Canada; ANAC in Argentina/Brazil; equivalent national civil-aviation authorities in Mexico, Uruguay, Paraguay) [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/04/naaa-joins-agricultural-aviation-organizations-of-the-americas-with-a-letter-calling-for-modern-safe-drone-regulatory-frameworks/]

### Six specific regulatory asks in the letter

1. **Yield-to-manned-aircraft** codification — drones must give right of way to manned ag aircraft as a default airspace rule, not as a courtesy
2. **Strengthened regulatory frameworks for spraying drone operations** specifically — distinct from generic-UAS rules
3. **Operator licenses and certifications with mandatory insurance** — both pilot certification and operator-level liability coverage as preconditions for spray-drone operation
4. **Drone identification and electronic-traceability systems** — broadcast / receive-side architecture beyond Remote ID alone (per the letter's framing)
5. **Clear coordination protocols for low-altitude airspace** — formalized procedures for shared use of the low-altitude treatment-flight envelope
6. **Increased inspections and penalties for illegal or irregular operations** — enforcement teeth, not just rules on paper [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/04/naaa-joins-agricultural-aviation-organizations-of-the-americas-with-a-letter-calling-for-modern-safe-drone-regulatory-frameworks/]

### Underlying safety-trend data (NAAA 2025 industry survey)

- **20% of US aerial-application operators** (manned ag-aircraft pilots) reported **at least one unsafe interaction with a drone** during the **2025 growing season** [source: https://avweb.com/flight-safety/ag-aircraft-drone-encounters-increase-2025/]
- Trend line — **2023: 11% → 2024: 16% → 2025: 20%** of operators reporting an unsafe drone encounter (context: NAAA annually surveys members; the rate has roughly doubled in two seasons) [source: https://avweb.com/flight-safety/ag-aircraft-drone-encounters-increase-2025/]
- **Operational reality cited in the trend coverage**: it is "extremely difficult, if not impossible, for manned aircraft to see a drone while conducting aerial applications **10 feet off the ground at speeds of up to 140 mph**" [source: https://avweb.com/flight-safety/ag-aircraft-drone-encounters-increase-2025/]
- **NAAA-recommended drone-operator safety stack** (from the underlying right-of-way advisories that the June 4 letter formalizes): yield to manned aircraft; **monitor 122.925 MHz** (the ag-aviation frequency); **carry liability insurance**; **land immediately** if a low-flying ag aircraft is in the area; use visibility and tracking equipment; coordinate with local aerial-application operations before flying [source: https://www.brownfieldagnews.com/news/ag-drone-pilots-urged-to-yield-to-manned-aircraft/] [source: https://southeastagnet.com/2026/04/24/agricultural-aviation-drone-safety-awareness/]

### Complementary AgAir Update editorial — June 1, 2026

- **"Fly Safe: Managing Risk in the 2026 Ag Aviation Season"** (AgAir Update, June 1, 2026) independently and three days earlier articulated the same two-principle airspace-sharing framework that the NAAA hemispheric letter formalizes: (1) drones must always yield right-of-way to manned aircraft; (2) manned aircraft should circle the field before entering an application site [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/01/fly-safe-managing-risk-in-the-2026-ag-aviation-season/]
- **Ferry-altitude modernization recommendation**: the legacy "ferry above five and stay alive" rule (500 ft AGL) is updated — modern turbine ag aircraft frequently maneuver above 500 ft AGL, so ferrying **closer to 1,000 ft AGL** is now recommended in many regions for a safer margin from drone treatment-flight altitudes [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/01/fly-safe-managing-risk-in-the-2026-ag-aviation-season/]
- **TFR / NOTAM compliance** flagged as a Part 137 ag-aviation responsibility — Flight Service Stations or 1800wxbrief.com cited as the recommended pre-flight briefing source, with proof of briefing retained for compliance purposes (context: this aligns with the FAA Compliance & Enforcement Bulletin **C&E Bulletin 2026-1** "legal action by default" posture; ag operators carrying documentation are insulated from the **DETER**-program first-time-violator enforcement track — see [2026-05-04 digest](2026-05-04.md))

### Context — prior 2026 growing-season alert (April 20, 2026)

- (context: NAAA's **April 20, 2026** growing-season alert was the precursor to the June 4 hemispheric letter; the April piece urged drone operators to give right of way to manned ag aircraft as a US-only ask) [source: https://www.americanagnetwork.com/2026/04/20/ag-aviation-industry-urges-drone-operators-to-give-right-of-way-to-manned-agricultural-aircraft-this-growing-season/] [source: https://www.croplife.com/iron/ag-aviation-industry-urges-all-drone-operators-to-operate-safely-and-give-right-of-way-to-manned-agricultural-aircraft/]
- The escalation from a US-only NAAA right-of-way advisory in April to a **seven-organization, six-country joint regulatory ask** in June is the substantive policy shift the dealer pitch should track — this is no longer a manned-ag-aviation safety appeal, it is a multi-country regulatory-architecture request lined up against drone operators who lack the certificated-operator posture the letter is naming

### Caveats — what the letter does NOT establish

- **No specific rule has changed** at the FAA, Transport Canada, or any signatory-country regulator as a result of this letter — it is an industry-trade-body policy ask, not a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking [source: https://agairupdate.com/2026/06/04/naaa-joins-agricultural-aviation-organizations-of-the-americas-with-a-letter-calling-for-modern-safe-drone-regulatory-frameworks/]
- **No timeline** is set for any of the six asks to be considered or implemented — civil-aviation authorities have not publicly responded as of this digest
- **The FAA's existing architecture already covers most of the asks** for the certificated population: Part 137 = registration + certification; Part 137 commercial coverage = mandatory insurance for the operating entity (though not for every Part 107 pilot individually); Remote ID + ADS-B In = drone identification + traceability (partial); 14 CFR § 91.115 already establishes right-of-way for aircraft engaged in agricultural operations — meaning the gap the letter targets is **enforcement and the un-certificated population**, not new rules for certificated Part 137 spray-drone operators
- **The 20% / 16% / 11% trend statistic** is a **survey-of-NAAA-members result, not an FAA incident-data result** — survey-design caveats apply (self-reporting, sample frame is manned-ag-aviation operators only); the magnitude of the trend is still a meaningful signal but should not be cited as an FAA-validated incident rate

## Implications

- **For dealer onboarding scripts and prospect conversations**: the NAAA + Americas coalition letter is the **first publicly-aligned manned-aviation regulatory ask** that explicitly distinguishes the certificated-spray-drone-operator pathway from the un-certificated drone-operator pathway. Lead with the certificated-operator posture as a structural advantage when discussing the Part 137 / Part 107 / Remote ID / liability-insurance stack — frame it as "the regulatory weather over the next 24 months is going to ask this of every spray-drone operator across the hemisphere; we set you up there from day one through the [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md)." Do NOT claim that any specific rule will change — none has been proposed.
- **For Sam Welton's [compliance program](../../content/compliance-program.md) curriculum**: this is a directly curriculum-relevant item. Add the NAAA April 20 → June 4 escalation as the **why-this-matters** framing for the Part 107 + Part 137 sequence, and explicitly walk students through the six asks the hemispheric letter makes — they map almost 1:1 to what a Part 137 + Part 107 + insured + Remote-ID-equipped operator already demonstrates. The teaching point: "the certification you are earning is what seven countries' agricultural-aviation trade bodies are now publicly asking civil-aviation regulators to require for all spray-drone operations. You are not behind the regulatory curve — you are ahead of it." Also add the **122.925 MHz ag-frequency monitoring** practice and the **land-immediately-if-low-flying-aircraft-is-in-the-area** rule to the operational-conduct curriculum module if not already there — both are explicit NAAA-recommended drone-operator practices that the certificated dealer customer should know.
- **For dealer detail-page content** ([products/i-19.md](../../products/i-19.md) and [products/r-32.md](../../products/r-32.md) "Product detail page content" sections, per the 2026-05-24 i19 ingest follow-up): the right-of-way / Remote ID / 122.925 MHz monitoring posture is a **platform-level beat** that fits naturally alongside the "Service Reports" and "owner-maintainable / right-to-repair" beats already filed there. The frame is **operator-side capability** rather than airframe-side capability — what a Terraplex-certificated operator does that a hobbyist drone operator does not.
- **For onboarding conversations with brokers / insurance carriers**: the NAAA letter's explicit ask for **mandatory operator liability insurance** for spray drones is a useful **third-party-validation talking point** when discussing the existing Part 137 commercial insurance requirement with a prospect who has not yet bought a policy. Honest framing: "the trade bodies for manned ag aviation across seven countries are publicly asking regulators to make this insurance mandatory for every spray-drone operator. Whether or not any specific rule changes, this is where the market is heading — your existing Part 137 carrier relationship is value, not overhead."
- **For monitoring follow-up**: watch (a) any **FAA response** to the letter — the FAA Administrator's office has not publicly responded as of June 7, 2026, but a public statement would be material; (b) any **Transport Canada or PMRA** response (Canada's parallel **Regulatory Proposal PRO2026-01**, with consultation closed March 25, 2026, may converge with the NAAA hemispheric letter's asks in its final form — potentially in time for the 2026 late-season or 2027 season); (c) any state-level (Iowa / Texas / Nebraska / Kansas / Missouri) enforcement uptick targeting un-certificated spray-drone operations during the 2026 growing season — this is where the "increased inspections and penalties" ask is most likely to land first in the US, and the [research/regulatory/2026-04-update.md](../regulatory/2026-04-update.md) rollup is the natural place to log it; (d) the **2026 NAAA industry survey** results (likely Q4 2026 or Q1 2027) to see whether the 11% → 16% → 20% trend continues — the survey's predictive value is rising as the rate climbs.
