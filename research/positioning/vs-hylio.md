## TL;DR

Hylio is also a US-based ag drone company and not on the FCC Covered List. When a farmer is comparing Revolution and Hylio, the conversation shifts from regulatory compliance (both pass) to sales model, support depth, and throughput. Revolution's advantages are the local dealer model, deeper compliance infrastructure (Sam Welton, Part 137, Part 107), and the R-32's significantly higher single-airframe daily coverage. Hylio's advantages are an FAA-approved swarm exemption and a lower entry price point. The honest pitch: both are good American choices. Revolution wins on local support depth and raw throughput. Hylio wins on swarm throughput and entry pricing.

## Objection Handlers

### "But Hylio is cheaper on entry"

The AG-230 is around $31K without kit. The I-19 starts at $46,250. That gap is real. The I-19 covers 600 acres/day with a 20-gallon tank, RTK precision, and 360° radar. The AG-230 covers 50 acres/hr with an 8-gallon tank at 35-foot swath. For high-volume operations, the I-19 does more per day. Run the numbers per acre over a season, then factor in dealer support access, compliance program value, and the compliance program's Part 107 cost ($449 vs sourcing it yourself). The sticker gap narrows when you price the total package.

### "But Hylio's swarm means more productivity"

Hylio's FAA exemption lets one operator run three drones at once — roughly 150 acres/hr. The R-32 covers 120-160 acres/hr on its own with one airframe to insure, license, and maintain. For custom applicators building a labor-efficient spray service business, the swarm is a genuine advantage. For farmers buying one drone for their own operation, the simpler answer is usually one reliable machine with high capacity. Ask the farmer which situation they're in.

### "Hylio has been around longer in the US"

Fair. Hylio shipped before Revolution's US scale-up. Being early to market matters for some buyers. It also means Hylio built their product before the FCC Covered List changed the landscape. Revolution was founded by a farmer who kept asking DJI for features and didn't get them. The product reflects that. Both have track records. The question is which one is better matched to this farmer's operation.

### "Hylio claims their hardware is NDAA-compliant"

Hylio markets the ARES as "NDAA compliant hardware." Their batteries are still China-sourced as of June 2026. KULR Technology Group and Hylio have a joint development agreement (February 2026) to build NDAA-compliant battery packs in Texas — but no domestic battery is shipping yet. Revolution/GTEEX is NDAA-natively-compliant by design, with components from NC, NE, IN, and IL, assembled in NC and KS/NE. For federal accounts or government-adjacent contracts, the distinction matters. For a private farmer, it's a values question — but don't accept Hylio's NDAA claim at face value without asking about the batteries.

## What Hylio Does Better

- **Swarm capability.** One Hylio operator can legally run three spray drones simultaneously (FAA exemption) plus fly after dark. No equivalent Revolution authorization exists. For custom applicators, this is a real labor multiplier.
- **Lower entry price.** The AG-230 at ~$31K is the most accessible US-made spray drone on the market. Farms not yet ready for an R-32 investment have a closer-to-DJI entry point.
- **AgFunder-backed scale.** Hylio's $2M StartEngine raise targets 5,000+ drones/year capacity. More production volume can mean faster parts availability at scale.
- **Newer product lineup.** The 2025 launch of ARES, ACRA, and PHOTON platforms shows active product development. Hylio is mid-refresh; Revolution's current products are the R-32 and I-19.
