## TL;DR

The US ag-drone market is restructuring around the FCC's December 22, 2025 Covered List ruling. Chinese OEMs (DJI, XAG) cannot get new US equipment authorizations. Three domestic or domestic-compliant platforms now divide the "safe to buy new" market: Revolution/GTEEX (NDAA-natively-compliant), Hylio (US-engineered with a China battery gap closing), and Elevon Aerial Ag (US-assembled under a time-limited Conditional Approval through December 2026). The dealer-certified-operator model is becoming a structural competitive advantage as safety enforcement tightens and compliance requirements grow.

## US Ag-Drone Market Dynamics

### Size and Growth

- US commercial drone market projected to reach $31.34 billion by 2034.
- 2025 performance: +58.7% acres sprayed by drone, -59% unit count. Operators are consolidating to fewer, larger, higher-throughput platforms.
- DJI global fleet: 600,000+ spray drones across 100+ countries, 500M+ hectares treated. DJI's stated 2026 US revenue exposure from the FCC ruling: ~$1.5B.
- US market absorption estimate: 8,000-10,000 spray drones per year at scale (industry analyst figure from post-FCC commentary).
- **Breakeven for farmer-owned operation: ~980 acres/year** at $12.27/acre all-in cost. Custom applicators break even faster at $7.39/acre operating cost. Payback period: 1-2 seasons for most operators. (Source: MU Extension G1274 — WebFetch 403, figure from search snippet.)
- Key growth driver: drones are now operating where ground rigs can't — wet soil, narrow spray windows, hilly terrain. Not replacing tractors. Adding coverage capability.

### The Three-Tier Domestic Landscape

| Tier | Platform | US Manufacturing Claim | FCC Status | Key Caveat |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Revolution/GTEEX** (R-32, I-19) | NC/NE/IN/IL components; NC + KS/NE assembly. NDAA-natively-compliant by design | Not on Covered List. Full authorization | No FAA swarm exemption |
| 2 | **Hylio** (ARES HYL-150, ATLAS, AG-230) | Designed and built in Richmond TX | Not on Covered List. Full authorization | Batteries still China-sourced; KULR TX battery JDA in progress, not shipping |
| 3 | **Elevon Aerial Ag** (Z30/Z50/Z80) | US-assembled outside St. Louis. EFT-derived airframes. Targeting >65% US-manufactured components | DoW Conditional Approval through Dec 31, 2026 | Time-limited and renewable. Does not affect Blue UAS or federal procurement eligibility |

The Conditional Approval mechanism (first established March 2026, second batch May 2026) is a new FCC/DoW tool. It creates an exception for US-assembled platforms using foreign-derived airframes under active DoD oversight. It terminates each year-end and must be renewed. This is a meaningful distinction for fleet planning: Revolution and Hylio have permanent full authorization; Elevon Aerial's status resets annually.

## Regulatory Landscape Summary

### FCC Covered List

The December 22, 2025 FCC Order added all foreign-manufactured UAS and critical components to the Covered List. Key architecture:

- **Existing authorized equipment**: legal to sell and operate. Not retroactively affected.
- **New equipment authorizations**: blocked for all foreign-manufactured UAS and critical components.
- **Firmware waiver (DA 26-454, May 8, 2026)**: Previously-authorized foreign UAS can receive software and firmware updates through at least January 1, 2029. Broadened to Class II permissive changes (substantive functional improvements, not just security patches).
- **Petitions for Reconsideration**: DJI (ET Docket 26-22) and Autel (ET Docket 26-23) petition reply windows closed May 11, 2026. FCC Commissioner Trusty publicly defended the ruling in May 2026. No decision timeline set. Reversal at Commission level appears unlikely based on public record.
- **Conditional Approvals**: Time-limited DoW exceptions for US-assembled platforms. Current holders: Elevon Aerial Ag (Z30/Z50/Z80, through Dec 31, 2026) and Air6 Systems.

### NDAA / Section 805

- **Section 805 direct procurement ban**: Effective June 30, 2026 for federal contractors. Covered List companies (DJI, XAG, Autel) cannot be directly procured by federal agencies.
- **Section 1260H indirect-supplier ban**: June 30, 2027. Covers supply chain links, not just direct procurement.
- **Dealer implication**: Any dealer serving USDA programs, FSA accounts, or government-adjacent customers should have already briefed them on the procurement timeline. Revolution/GTEEX is not implicated in either ban.

### FAA Regulatory

- **Part 137**: Agricultural Aircraft Operations certificate. Unchanged. The regulatory standard for commercial aerial application. Terraplex's compliance program (Sam Welton) includes full Part 137 support — this is a structural advantage that most drone sellers cannot match.
- **Section 2209 NPRM** (FAA-2026-4558, May 6, 2026): Proposes new 14 CFR Part 74 — Unmanned Aircraft-Free Zones over fixed-site critical infrastructure. Part 137 transit carve-out preserved at §74.250 with Remote ID + facility notification. Comments due July 6, 2026.
- **FAA DETER program** (effective April 17, 2026): Fast-track settlement for first-time small-UAS violators. Applies to farmer and ag-service operators. Certified operators with clean compliance records are the defensible operational standard.
- **World Cup TFRs** (June 1-July 21, 2026): 100+ team base-camp rings (1 nm / surface-to-400 ft AGL) continuously active in non-host cities including Louisville KY and others in Terraplex territory. Dealers in affected geographies should brief active operators.
- **NAAA 2026 safety enforcement tone**: FAA FAR McIntosh's framing — "we see drone operators as pilots" — signals a shift toward treating spray-drone operators with the same compliance expectations as manned applicators.

### Airspace Safety — Emerging Enforcement Trend

NAAA's 2026 industry survey: **20% of US manned aerial applicators reported at least one unsafe drone encounter in 2025**, up from 16% in 2024 and 11% in 2023. Roughly doubled in three seasons.

NAAA and the FAA response:
- Codifying drone yield-to-manned-aircraft right-of-way
- Pushing for mandatory drone registration and insurance for commercial operators
- Recommending ADS-B In as default on heavy spray drones in corn-belt territories
- Tougher enforcement posture (DETER program, penalty escalation)

**For dealers**: This is a tailwind for certified operators. Terraplex's compliance program (Part 137 + Part 107 + Remote ID + commercial liability insurance guidance) already addresses the full NAAA recommended practice set. Dealers who can point farmers to a compliance program aren't just upselling — they're managing their customer's regulatory exposure.

June 2026 addition: NAAA joined six other national agricultural-aviation associations (US, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Uruguay, Mexico, Paraguay) in a joint letter to regulators across all seven countries calling for enforceable drone safety frameworks. First multi-country alignment of this kind. Reinforces the long-term direction: commercial drone operations require commercial-grade compliance.

## Key Trends for Dealer Positioning (Mid-2026)

**1. Regulatory durability is the headline.**

The FCC ruling has DoD opposition, FCC Commissioner public defense, Ninth Circuit in abeyance, and no Commission vote scheduled. The "Built in the Americas" positioning is not contingent on a political outcome — it reflects the current regulatory architecture. Dealers who treated this as temporary are running out of runway.

**2. Throughput consolidation favors R-32.**

Farmers are buying fewer, larger drones. The unit-count drop (-59% in 2025) with acreage up (+58.7%) means operators are moving to higher-capacity platforms. The R-32's 32-gallon tank and 1,200-1,600 acres/day coverage positions it in the consolidation's winning tier. The I-19's 600 acres/day is the right answer for smaller operations or operators who want multi-role flexibility (spray, spread, scout).

**3. Certified operators are the defensible standard.**

Tighter airspace enforcement, DETER, NAAA campaigns, and the multi-nation coalition letter all push toward a commercial-grade compliance expectation. Dealers who help farmers get and maintain Part 137 and Part 107 certification are building stickier customer relationships than dealers who just move hardware.

**4. Right-to-repair matters more than before.**

DJI's locked ecosystem and foreign OEM supply chain uncertainty make Revolution's open technical manual posture a concrete selling point. The tech manual foreword explicitly says the platform is designed for owner and dealer repair. This is a real differentiator, not marketing language.

**5. Dealer model vs direct is becoming a purchasing criterion.**

As the market matures, farmers who bought direct-from-OEM are learning the support gap. The NAAA's compliance-focus trend makes "who supports my operation" a more urgent question. A local dealer who stocks parts, handles compliance, and runs the same equipment is increasingly the reason a sale gets made.

**6. New domestic entrants are widening the field.**

Elevon Aerial Ag, SiFly (Santa Clara, NDAA-compliant Q250), Exedy Drones (Michigan, Japanese-owned Automotive engineering), and Ceres Air (Vermont) are all entering or scaling in 2026. Revolution's advantages are dealer depth, established compliance infrastructure, and NDAA-native design — not first-mover position alone. The story will need to stay current.
